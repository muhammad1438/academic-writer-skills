# Plain-Language Summary

Plain-language summaries (also called lay summaries, plain-language abstracts, or public summaries) translate research for non-expert audiences. Unlike academic writing, they prioritise accessibility, avoid jargon, and connect findings to real-world relevance. Many funders now require them as a condition of grant funding.

---

## What Is a Lay Summary?

**Purpose:** Enable a non-specialist audience to understand what you did, what you found, and why it matters.

**Audience:** Public, patients, policymakers, media, school students, or interdisciplinary colleagues.

**Length:** Typically 100–250 words, though requirements vary.

**Key difference from abstracts:** Abstracts summarise for experts; lay summaries explain for non-experts.

---

## Funder Requirements

### Wellcome Trust

**Requirement:** Lay summary required for all grants.

**Length:** Up to 400 characters (very short) for application; longer summary may be required for dissemination.

**Audience:** General public.

**Key guidance:** Explain what you're doing and why it's important without using technical language.

### NIHR (National Institute for Health Research)

**Requirement:** Plain English summary required.

**Length:** 250 words maximum.

**Audience:** Patients, public, healthcare professionals.

**Criteria:**
- Clear and jargon-free
- Explains why the research matters to patients
- Uses active voice

### EU Horizon Europe

**Requirement:** Lay summary for each published output.

**Length:** 1,000 characters minimum (~150 words).

**Content:** What the project is about, main results, and potential impact.

**Audience:** Citizens, policymakers, media.

### NHMRC (Australia)

**Requirement:** Plain language summary required.

**Length:** 150–200 words.

**Audience:** General public.

### NIH (US)

**Requirement:** Public access compliance; lay summaries increasingly expected.

**Trend:** NIH has moved toward requiring plain-language summaries of funded research.

## Journal Requirements

### PLOS ONE

**Requirement:** Author Summary (optional but encouraged).

**Length:** 150–200 words.

**Audience:** Educated non-specialists.

**Format:** Answer three questions:
1. Why was this study done?
2. What did the researchers do and find?
3. What do these findings mean?

### Nature Family Journals

**Requirement:** Plain-language summary (at author's initiative or editorial invitation).

**Audience:** General readers, media.

### BMJ

**Requirement:** Patient summary for research articles.

**Length:** 150–250 words.

**Audience:** Patients and public.

**Format:** Co-produced with patient partners where possible.

## Audience Considerations

| Audience | Focus | Tone |
|---|---|---|
| **General public** | Relevance to daily life; societal impact | Conversational, clear |
| **Patients** | Implications for care, treatment, quality of life | Empathetic, empowering |
| **Policymakers** | Evidence for policy decisions; cost-benefit | Concise, actionable |
| **Media** | Newsworthiness; human interest | Compelling, quotable |
| **School students** | Curiosity, career relevance | Engaging, educational |

## Readability Targets

### Recommended Grade Levels

| Audience | Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level |
|---|---|
| General public | 8–10 (13–15 year-old reading level) |
| Patients / health information | 6–8 (11–13 year-old reading level) |
| Policymakers | 10–12 |
| Media | 8–10 |

### Readability Formulas

| Metric | Formula Focus | Target Score |
|---|---|---|
| **Flesch Reading Ease** | Sentence length + syllable count | 60–70 (standard) |
| **Flesch-Kincaid Grade Level** | Years of education needed | 8–10 |
| **SMOG** | Complex words | Grade 8–10 |
| **Gunning Fog** | Sentence length + complex words | 8–10 |

### How to Check Readability

- **Microsoft Word:** Review → Editor → Document Stats
- **Hemingway Editor:** Free online tool (hemingwayapp.com)
- **Readable:** Online readability checker
- **Grammarly:** Includes readability score

## Writing Techniques

### Avoid Jargon

| Jargon | Plain Language |
|---|---|
| "Aetiology" | Cause |
| "Efficacy" | How well it works |
| "Morbidity" | Illness |
| "Mortality" | Death |
| "Intervention" | Treatment or programme |
| "Modifiable risk factor" | Something you can change |
| "Longitudinal study" | Study over time |
| "Cohort" | Group |
| "Statistically significant" | Clear / meaningful (explain what it means practically) |
| "In vivo" | In living organisms |

### Use Active Voice

**Passive (avoid):** "A study was conducted to investigate the effects of..."
**Active (prefer):** "We investigated the effects of..."

### Keep Sentences Short

**Target:** Average 15–20 words per sentence.

**Long (avoid):** "Participants who were randomly assigned to the intervention group, which consisted of a 12-week exercise programme delivered three times weekly, demonstrated significant improvements in cardiovascular fitness compared to those in the control group who received no intervention."
**Short (prefer):** "Participants were randomly assigned to two groups. The intervention group did a 12-week exercise programme, three times a week. They improved their cardiovascular fitness more than the control group."

### Use Concrete Examples

**Abstract (avoid):** "This intervention may reduce cardiovascular disease risk in high-risk populations."
**Concrete (prefer):** "This programme could help people at high risk of heart disease — such as those with diabetes or high blood pressure — lower their chances of a heart attack."

### Explain Technical Terms If Necessary

If you must use a technical term, explain it:

> "We measured HbA1c, a blood test that shows average blood sugar levels over the past three months."

## Structure

### The 4-Move Structure

| Move | Content | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Hook** | Why should the reader care? | "Heart disease kills more people in the UK than any other condition." |
| **What we did** | What was the study? | "We tested whether a simple blood test could predict heart attacks before they happen." |
| **What we found** | Key finding(s) | "We found that the test correctly identified 8 out of 10 people who would have a heart attack within five years." |
| **Why it matters** | Implications | "This test could help doctors identify high-risk patients early and offer preventive treatment." |

## Templates

### 100-Word Lay Summary

```
[Hook — 1 sentence]
[What we did — 1–2 sentences]
[What we found — 1–2 sentences]
[Why it matters — 1 sentence]
```

**Example:**
> Depression affects 1 in 5 people at some point in their lives. We investigated whether regular exercise could reduce symptoms of mild to moderate depression. In a study of 300 adults, those who exercised for 30 minutes three times a week reported a 40% reduction in symptoms compared to those who did not exercise. This suggests that exercise could be an effective, low-cost treatment option for depression, alongside therapy and medication.

### 250-Word Summary

```
[Hook — 1–2 sentences establishing significance]

[What we did — 2–3 sentences explaining the study in accessible terms]

[What we found — 3–4 sentences presenting key findings, with concrete numbers where possible]

[Why it matters — 2–3 sentences on implications for patients, public, or policy]

[Limitation and next steps — optional, 1–2 sentences]
```

**Example:**
> Dementia affects over 55 million people worldwide, with no cure currently available. Identifying the condition early could help people access support sooner and plan for the future.
>
> We investigated whether a simple memory test could detect the earliest signs of dementia, years before symptoms become obvious. We tested 500 adults aged 60–75, half of whom had a family history of dementia. Participants took a 10-minute word recall test, which we compared to detailed brain scans.
>
> We found that people who scored poorly on the memory test were three times more likely to show early brain changes linked to dementia, even though they had no noticeable symptoms. The test correctly identified 75% of people who would develop dementia within five years.
>
> This simple, low-cost test could be offered in GP surgeries to identify people at high risk of dementia. Early identification means people can access support, make lifestyle changes, and participate in clinical trials of new treatments. We are now testing whether the test works in larger, more diverse populations.

### Press Release Format

```
**Headline:** [Active, newsworthy, accurate]

**Lead paragraph:** [Who, what, when, where, why — most newsworthy finding]

**Quote from researcher:** "..." [Adds credibility and human interest]

**Context:** [Why this matters; how it fits into the bigger picture]

**Methodology summary:** [Brief, accessible explanation of what was done]

**Key findings:** [Bulleted list for easy scanning]

**Implications:** [What happens next; how could this be used]

**Funding and affiliations**

**Contact information**
```

## Social Media Translation

### Twitter/X Thread

**Format:** 5–8 tweets telling the story of the research.

**Structure:**
1. Hook tweet with key finding
2. Why this topic matters
3. What we did
4. Key findings (2–3 tweets)
5. Why it matters
6. Link to full paper

**Example:**
> 🧵 THREAD: Our new study shows a simple blood test could predict heart attacks 5 years before they happen.
>
> Heart disease kills 1 in 4 people. Early detection could save lives. Here's what we found... [1/7]

### LinkedIn Post

**Format:** Professional tone, clear value proposition.

**Structure:**
- Hook headline
- 2–3 sentences on the problem
- Key finding (bold)
- Implications for practice or policy
- Call to action (read paper, get in touch)

### Blog Post

**Length:** 500–800 words.

**Structure:**
- Compelling title
- Personal hook
- Background and significance
- What we did
- What we found
- What it means
- Call to action or reflection

## Policy Brief Format

**Audience:** Policymakers who need evidence for decisions.

**Structure:**
1. **Key message** — One sentence summarising the policy implication
2. **The issue** — 2–3 sentences on the problem
3. **The evidence** — What the research found
4. **Recommendations** — Numbered, actionable policy recommendations
5. **References** — Key sources (3–5)

**Example:**
> **Key message:** Free school meals for all primary pupils could reduce childhood obesity by 15% and save the NHS £200 million annually.
>
> **The issue:** Childhood obesity has doubled in 30 years, with children from low-income families disproportionately affected...
>
> **The evidence:** Our analysis of 500 schools found that universal free school meals...
>
> **Recommendations:**
> 1. Extend universal free school meals to all primary schools
> 2. Set nutritional standards for school meals
> 3. Fund cooking and nutrition education

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Why It's a Problem | Correction |
|---|---|---|
| Patronising tone | Offends readers; undermines trust | Respect your audience's intelligence; just explain differently |
| Over-simplification | Loses nuance; can mislead | Simplify language, not findings |
| Missing the significance | Reader doesn't understand why it matters | Always answer: "So what?" |
| Using jargon "because it's precise" | Precision is lost if the reader doesn't understand | Explain in plain language; add technical term in parentheses if needed |
| Abstract-like structure | Experts and publics need different information | Lead with impact, not methods |
| No concrete examples | Abstract concepts are hard to grasp | Use real-world analogies |
| Hiding limitations | Damages credibility | Acknowledge limitations briefly |
| Missing call to action | Reader doesn't know what to do with the information | Suggest next steps or implications |

## Testing Your Lay Summary

### Non-Expert Review

Ask someone outside your field to read it. If they can't summarise it back to you accurately, revise.

### Readability Check

Use tools to verify grade level is 8–10.

### Checklist

- [ ] No jargon (or jargon explained)
- [ ] Active voice used
- [ ] Sentences average 15–20 words
- [ ] Concrete examples provided
- [ ] Significance clearly stated
- [ ] Appropriate length for requirement
- [ ] Readability score within target range
