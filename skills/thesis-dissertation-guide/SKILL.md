# Thesis & Dissertation Guide

A thesis or dissertation is the capstone of a research degree — a sustained piece of scholarship demonstrating the candidate's ability to conduct independent research. Unlike journal articles, which are typically concise and focused, theses allow for comprehensive depth and must satisfy multiple stakeholders: supervisors, examiners, the institution, and the discipline.

---

## Thesis Types

### Monograph

**Structure:** A single, book-length manuscript with integrated chapters.

**Typical in:** Humanities, social sciences, UK/Europe PhDs.

**Characteristics:**
- Sustained argument across 60,000–100,000 words
- Unified narrative arc
- Comprehensive literature integration

### Article-Based (Cumulative / Publication-Based)

**Structure:** A compilation of published or submitted papers, with an overarching introduction and synthesis discussion.

**Typical in:** Sciences, engineering, medicine, increasingly common in social sciences.

**Characteristics:**
- 3–5 journal articles (published or manuscript-ready)
- General introduction (10,000–15,000 words)
- General discussion/conclusion (5,000–10,000 words)
- May be required or optional depending on institution

## Standard Chapter Structure by Discipline

### Humanities (Literature, History, Philosophy, Arts)

| Chapter | Typical Length | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | 5,000–10,000 | Hook, research questions, contribution, chapter outline |
| Literature Review | 10,000–20,000 | Critical engagement with key texts, historiography, theoretical frameworks |
| Theoretical Framework | 5,000–10,000 (may be separate) | Theoretical lens, key concepts, position in the field |
| Analysis Chapters (2–4) | 10,000–15,000 each | Close readings, archival work, case studies |
| Conclusion | 5,000–8,000 | Synthesis, contributions, limitations, future research |

**Total:** 70,000–100,000 words (UK PhD); 150,000–300,000 words (German/Habilitationschrift)

### Sciences (Biology, Chemistry, Physics, Engineering)

| Chapter | Typical Length | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | 5,000–8,000 | Background, rationale, aims, contribution |
| Literature Review | 8,000–12,000 | Field context, gap identification |
| Methods | 5,000–10,000 | Detailed methodology for reproducibility |
| Results Chapters (2–4) | 8,000–15,000 each | Data presentation, figures, tables |
| Discussion | 8,000–12,000 | Interpretation, implications, limitations |
| Conclusion | 3,000–5,000 | Summary, contributions, future directions |

**Total:** 60,000–80,000 words

### Social Sciences (Psychology, Sociology, Education, Political Science)

| Chapter | Typical Length | Content |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | 5,000–8,000 | Context, problem, aims, research questions, contribution |
| Literature Review | 10,000–15,000 | Theoretical traditions, empirical context, gap |
| Methodology | 8,000–12,000 | Research design, methods, sampling, ethics, positionality (if qualitative) |
| Findings/Results | 10,000–20,000 | Data presentation, organised by theme or RQ |
| Discussion | 8,000–12,000 | Interpretation, theoretical contribution, implications |
| Conclusion | 3,000–5,000 | Summary, limitations, recommendations |

**Total:** 70,000–100,000 words

## Title Page and Preliminaries

### Title Page Elements
- Full thesis title
- Candidate name
- Degree name
- Department and institution
- Date of submission
- Supervisor name(s)

### Preliminary Pages (in order)

| Page | Content |
|---|---|
| **Abstract** | 250–500 words; summary of research question, methods, findings, contribution |
| **Acknowledgements** | Personal and professional acknowledgements |
| **Table of Contents** | All chapters, sections, and subsections with page numbers |
| **List of Figures** | Figure number, title, page |
| **List of Tables** | Table number, title, page |
| **List of Abbreviations** | If extensive technical abbreviations used |
| **Declaration** | Statement of originality; confirmation of ethical approval |

## Introduction Chapter

### Structure

**Paragraph 1 — The Hook**
Open with the significance of the topic. Why does it matter? Who cares?

**Paragraphs 2–4 — Context and Background**
Establish the field, the key debates, and the problem your thesis addresses.

**The Gap**
What is missing in our understanding? What has not been studied? Why is this gap significant?

**Research Aims and Questions**
State your aims clearly. If you have research questions, list them. If you have hypotheses, state them.

**Theoretical Framework (Brief)**
Signal the theoretical lens — expanded in later chapter.

**Methodology (Brief)**
Brief indication of approach — detailed in methods chapter.

**Contribution**
What does this thesis offer? Who benefits?

**Chapter Outline**
One paragraph per chapter, summarising its purpose.

### Opening Example
> Climate change is reshaping agricultural systems worldwide, with smallholder farmers in the Global South bearing disproportionate impacts. Yet the mechanisms by which these farmers adapt — and the barriers they face — remain poorly understood. This thesis examines adaptation strategies among smallholder coffee farmers in Colombia, asking: What factors enable or constrain adaptive capacity? How do farmers perceive and respond to climate risks? What role do institutional support systems play? Through 18 months of ethnographic fieldwork in three regions, I argue that adaptive capacity is fundamentally shaped by social networks and trust — factors that existing policy frameworks overlook.

## Literature Review Chapter

### Purpose

Not a summary of "everything written" on the topic. Instead: a **critical synthesis** that positions your research within existing knowledge.

### Organisation Options

| Organisation | When to Use |
|---|---|
| **Thematic** | Organise by themes or concepts across the literature |
| **Chronological** | Trace the development of an idea over time |
| **Methodological** | Organise by research approaches and their contributions |
| **Theoretical** | Organise by competing theoretical frameworks |
| **Problem-focused** | Organise around aspects of the research problem |

### Key Moves

1. **Map the field** — What are the key debates? Who are the main voices?
2. **Identify the gap** — What is missing? What is under-researched?
3. **Position your research** — How does your thesis contribute?

### Integration vs. Summary

**Summary (AVOID):**
> Smith (2018) found X. Jones (2019) found Y. Brown (2020) found Z.

**Integration (PREFER):**
> Scholars have debated the causes of X for decades. Smith (2018) argues that economic factors are primary, while Jones (2019) emphasises cultural influences. Brown (2020) offers a synthesis, suggesting that these factors interact. However, none of these studies considers the role of [your focus] — a gap this thesis addresses.

## Theoretical Framework Chapter

### When to Include

- Humanities and theory-heavy social sciences: typically a separate chapter
- Sciences and applied fields: may be integrated into literature review or introduction

### Purpose

To articulate the theoretical lens through which you analyse your data — the concepts, assumptions, and analytical tools that shape your interpretation.

### Elements

1. **Theory introduction** — What is the theory? Who developed it?
2. **Key concepts** — Define and explain the central concepts
3. **Application to your topic** — How does this theory illuminate your research problem?
4. **Limitations** — What does this theory not explain?

---

## Methodology Chapter

### Purpose

To justify and describe how you conducted your research, with enough detail for replication.

### Elements

| Element | Content |
|---|---|
| **Research Design** | What type of study (experimental, survey, ethnographic, case study)? Why this design? |
| **Philosophical Foundations** | Ontology and epistemology (particularly for qualitative work) |
| **Sampling Strategy** | Who participated? How were they selected? Justification. |
| **Data Collection Methods** | What methods (interviews, surveys, experiments)? Instruments? Procedure? |
| **Data Analysis** | How were data analysed? Software? Analytical approach? |
| **Ethics** | Ethical approval process, informed consent, data management |
| **Reflexivity (Qualitative)** | Researcher positionality, potential biases |
| **Limitations** | Acknowledge what the methodology cannot do |

### Sample Size Justification Examples

> A power analysis using G*Power indicated that a minimum sample of 128 participants was required to detect a medium effect size (d = 0.5) with 80% power at α = 0.05. We recruited 150 participants to account for potential attrition.

> Consistent with grounded theory principles, we used theoretical sampling and continued recruitment until theoretical saturation was reached at 22 interviews, defined as no new categories emerging across three consecutive interviews.

## Results/Findings Chapters

### Organisation Options

| Organisation | When to Use |
|---|---|
| **By research question** | Each chapter addresses one RQ |
| **By theme** | Qualitative research; each chapter is a major theme |
| **By study** | Article-based thesis; each paper is a chapter |
| **By experiment** | Lab-based sciences; each experiment is a chapter |
| **Chronological** | Historical or developmental research |

### Structure Within Each Chapter

1. **Introduction** — What this chapter addresses
2. **Findings** — Data presentation (figures, tables, quotations)
3. **Brief interpretation** — What the findings mean (full interpretation in Discussion)

---

## Discussion Chapter

### Purpose

To interpret your findings, connect them to existing literature, and articulate your contribution.

### Structure

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| **Summary of Findings** | Brief restatement of key findings |
| **Interpretation** | What do the findings mean? Why might they have occurred? |
| **Relation to Literature** | How do findings confirm, extend, or challenge existing knowledge? |
| **Theoretical Contribution** | What does this mean for theory? |
| **Practical Implications** | Who can use these findings? How? |
| **Limitations** | Honest acknowledgment of what the study cannot claim |
| **Future Research** | What questions remain? What should others study? |

### Discussion vs. Results

**Results:** "We found that X was associated with Y (β = 0.45, p < 0.01)."

**Discussion:** "The association between X and Y suggests that [interpretation]. This extends Smith's (2018) finding by showing that the relationship holds in [new context]. A possible mechanism is [explanation]."

---

## Conclusion Chapter

### Purpose

To synthesise the thesis's contribution and signal its significance.

### Elements

1. **Summary** — Restate research questions, methods, and key findings (2–3 pages)
2. **Contributions** — What does this thesis contribute to knowledge? To practice? To policy?
3. **Reflections** — Personal reflections on the research journey (optional but common in UK)
4. **Future Directions** — What remains unanswered? What would you do differently?
5. **Closing Statement** — Final sentence that resonates

## Voice and Tense Conventions

| Chapter | Voice | Tense |
|---|---|---|
| Introduction | Active ("I argue...") | Present for claims, past for what you did |
| Literature Review | Mix | Present for others' arguments |
| Methodology | Mix | Past for what you did |
| Results | Passive for methods ("Data were collected..."); active for interpretation | Past for completed actions; present for what tables/figures show |
| Discussion | Active | Present for interpretation |
| Conclusion | Active | Present for contributions, past for summary of findings |

## Examiner Expectations

### UK Viva (Oral Examination)

**Format:** 1–3 hours with 1–2 examiners (plus independent chair).

**What examiners assess:**
1. Did you do the work yourself?
2. Do you understand what you did and why?
3. Is the work original?
4. Does it make a significant contribution?
5. Is it publishable?

**Outcomes:**
- Pass without corrections (rare)
- Pass with minor corrections (common)
- Pass with major corrections
- Revise and resubmit
- Fail

### US Defense

**Format:** Public presentation followed by Q&A with committee.

**What examiners assess:**
1. Command of the field
2. Rigor of methodology
3. Quality of analysis
4. Contribution to the discipline
5. Ability to defend the work orally

**Outcomes:**
- Pass
- Pass with revisions
- Fail

| Country | Word Count | Format | Examination |
|---|---|---|---|
| **UK** | 70,000–100,000 (PhD); 40,000–60,000 (Masters) | Monograph or article-based | Viva voce (oral exam) |
| **US** | No fixed limit (typically 150–300 pages) | Monograph (chapters) | Defense (public presentation + committee Q&A) |

| **Germany** | ~100,000 (monograph) | Monograph | Disputation (oral defense) |
| **EU (varies)** | Varies by country; Bologna Process standardising | Varies | Varies; many require publication |

## Timeline and Milestone Management

### Typical 3-Year PhD Timeline (UK)

| Stage | Timeline | Activities |
|---|---|---|
| **Year 1** | Months 1–12 | Literature review, methods development, ethics approval, pilot study |
| **Year 2** | Months 13–24 | Data collection, analysis, conference presentations, begin drafting chapters |
| **Year 3** | Months 25–36 | Writing, revisions, submission, viva preparation |
| **Post-submission** | Months 37–42 | Corrections, resubmission, final approval |

### Key Milestones

1. **First Year Review / Upgrade** — From MPhil to PhD registration
2. **Ethics Approval** — Before data collection
3. **Data Collection Complete** — End of Year 2 typically
4. **First Full Draft** — Month 30
5. **Complete Draft to Supervisors** — Month 33
6. **Submission** — Month 36
7. **Viva / Defense** — 1–3 months after submission
8. **Corrections Submitted** — 1–6 months after viva

## Working with Supervisors

### Communication

- Agree on meeting frequency (weekly, fortnightly, monthly)
- Prepare agendas; send work in advance
- Take notes; follow up with summary email

### Feedback Cycles

- Allow 2–4 weeks for supervisor feedback on chapters
- Respond to all comments; document your responses
- Track versions carefully (e.g., "Ch3_draft4_2026-03-16")

### Managing Disagreements

- Supervisors' advice is guidance, not command
- You are the expert on your specific project
- If disagreement persists, seek second opinion or departmental support

## Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Correction |
|---|---|
| No clear research question | Define RQ early; keep it visible |
| Literature review as summary | Synthesise; show relationships between sources |
| Methods chapter too brief | Provide enough detail for replication |
| Results without interpretation | Don't just present data; explain its significance |
| Discussion that only repeats results | Interpret; connect to literature; articulate contribution |
| Overclaiming contribution | Be specific; avoid "this thesis will change the field" |
| Ignoring supervisor feedback | Address all comments; document responses |
| Poor time management | Work backwards from submission; set internal deadlines |
| Inconsistent formatting | Use styles; create a style guide for yourself |
| Inadequate proofreading | Allow 2–4 weeks for final proofreading |
