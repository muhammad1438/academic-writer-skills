# Argumentation Engine

Academic argumentation is not rhetoric — it is the structured, evidence-based demonstration that a claim is well-founded. The choice of argumentation model shapes the structure, tone, and persuasive strategy of the entire paper.

---

## Three Dominant Argumentation Models

| Model | Primary Purpose | Best Used When | Audience |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Classical (Aristotelian)** | Persuade toward one correct answer | Clear dichotomy exists; issue has a right/wrong | Neutral or open-minded |
| **Toulmin** | Analyse and justify the logic connecting evidence to claim | Complex, nuanced debates; policy, science, ethics | Expert / Academic |
| **Rogerian** | Build consensus; find common ground | Highly polarised, emotionally charged topics | Hostile or defensive |

---

## 1. The Classical (Aristotelian) Model

The oldest and most universally recognised structure. Works best for issues with a clear correct answer or where one side is clearly stronger.

### Structure
1. **Introduction (Exordium):** Hook the reader + provide background + state the thesis clearly
2. **Narration (Narratio):** Provide the factual context the reader needs to understand the debate and its stakes
3. **Confirmation (Confirmatio):** Core body — logical points supported directly by evidence, presented from strongest to weakest (or weakest to strongest)
4. **Refutation (Refutatio):** Anticipate and systematically dismantle the strongest counterarguments
5. **Conclusion (Peroratio):** Restate thesis (reworded) + reinforce the position + call to action or broader implication

### The Three Aristotelian Appeals
- **Logos** — Appeal to logic and evidence (most important in academic writing)
- **Ethos** — Appeal to credibility and authority (citing established scholars, demonstrating expertise)
- **Pathos** — Appeal to emotion (used sparingly in academic writing; more prominent in policy or advocacy writing)

---

## 2. The Toulmin Model

Developed by philosopher Stephen Toulmin. The most analytically rigorous model — it makes **explicit** the logical chain that connects evidence to conclusion. Ideal for academic papers where assumptions must be surfaced and justified.

### The Six Components

**Claim** — The main assertion you are trying to prove
> *"Universities should mandate open-access publication for all publicly funded research."*

**Grounds (Data)** — The empirical evidence, facts, or data that support the claim
> *"Taxpayer-funded research is currently locked behind paywalls inaccessible to the majority of the public and to researchers in low-income countries."*

**Warrant** — The logical principle or assumption that connects the grounds to the claim (this is the most important and most neglected element)
> *"Research funded by public money should be accessible to the public that funded it."*

**Backing** — Additional support specifically for the warrant, not just for the claim
> *"The Budapest Open Access Initiative (2002) and UNESCO's Recommendation on Open Science (2021) both affirm the principle that publicly funded knowledge is a global public good."*

**Qualifier** — Language that limits the claim's scope; acknowledges it is not universally absolute
> *"In most cases," "Under typical conditions," "Where feasible," "With appropriate exceptions for sensitive research..."*

**Rebuttal (Exceptions)** — Specific conditions under which the claim would NOT hold
> *"This principle may not apply to research with genuine national security or commercial confidentiality implications."*

### Why the Warrant Is Critical
Without making the warrant explicit, the reader must assume the logical connection between evidence and claim. In academic writing, never leave the warrant implicit — state it and defend it. Weak academic arguments almost always have an unexamined warrant.

## 3. The Rogerian Model

Rooted in humanistic psychology (Carl Rogers). Prioritises empathy, trust, and consensus. Deliberately avoids adversarial framing. Most effective for politically or emotionally charged topics where the audience may be defensive.

### Structure
1. **Introduction:** Acknowledge the controversy neutrally — do not immediately state your position
2. **Opposing Viewpoint (Fairly Presented):** Present the opposing perspective fully and respectfully — validate the legitimate concerns of those who disagree; do not strawman
3. **Common Ground:** Identify shared values, goals, or concerns that both sides hold — establish that you and the opponent both want something worthwhile
4. **Your Position:** Introduce your thesis carefully, framing it as a reasonable position that incorporates and respects the opposition's concerns — not as a defeat of the opponent
5. **Conclusion:** Propose a synthesised solution that serves both parties' core interests

### When to Use Rogerian
- Gun control, immigration policy, abortion, euthanasia — any topic where readers have strong prior commitments
- When writing for a general audience rather than an academic expert one
- When the goal is to change behaviour or build coalition, not just to win the argument

## Building a Strong Thesis Statement

### Criteria for a Strong Academic Thesis
1. **Arguable** — A reasonable person could disagree
2. **Specific** — Makes a precise, bounded claim
3. **Significant** — The claim matters; it has stakes
4. **Supportable** — Evidence exists to back it up
5. **Original** — Goes beyond restating what is already widely agreed

| Thesis | Problem |
|---|---|
| "Social media has both positive and negative effects." | Not arguable — too neutral; states a truism |
| "This paper will discuss climate change policy." | Describes the paper, not an argument |
| "Social media is bad for teenagers." | Too vague; "bad" is undefined |
| "Algorithmic amplification of emotionally provocative content on social media platforms is a significant driver of political polarisation among adolescents." | ✓ Specific, arguable, significant, supportable |

## Counterargument and Rebuttal

Including and effectively defeating counterarguments **strengthens** an academic argument by demonstrating intellectual honesty and command of the debate.

### Concession-Refutation Formula
> "While it is true that [opposing point], [your refutation because / however / nevertheless]..."
> "Proponents of X argue that [counterargument]. However, this position overlooks [specific gap or weakness]."
> "This objection has merit insofar as [valid element], yet it fails to account for [your stronger evidence]."

### Types of Rebuttal
- **Denial:** The counter-evidence is factually wrong or methodologically flawed
- **Minimisation:** The opposing point is true but minor compared to the weight of evidence on your side
- **Incorporation:** The opposition's concern is valid and your argument already addresses it
- **Distinction:** The counterargument applies to a different situation, population, or time period than yours

## Evidence Types and Their Academic Weight

| Evidence Type | Strength | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Peer-reviewed empirical data | Highest | Primary source; must check methodology |
| Systematic reviews / meta-analyses | Very high | Gold standard for cumulative evidence |
| Secondary academic sources | High | Must be recent and relevant |
| Reports from credible organisations | Moderate–High | WHO, OECD, government bodies |
| Expert testimony | Moderate | Weight depends on the expert's field |
| Case studies | Moderate | Limited generalisability; strong for illustration |
| Anecdotal evidence | Low | Never use as primary evidence in academic writing |
| Unreviewed sources / blogs | Very low | Generally not acceptable |

## Logical Fallacies to Avoid in Academic Writing

| Fallacy | Description |
|---|---|
| **Ad hominem** | Attacking the person rather than the argument |
| **Straw man** | Misrepresenting the opponent's position to make it easier to attack |
| **False dichotomy** | Presenting only two options when more exist |
| **Hasty generalisation** | Drawing broad conclusions from insufficient data |
| **Post hoc** | Assuming causation from correlation or temporal sequence |
| **Appeal to authority** | Citing authority without engaging with their actual argument |
| **Circular reasoning** | Using the conclusion as a premise |
| **Slippery slope** | Asserting a chain of consequences without evidence for each link |

## PEEL Paragraph — Argumentation Application

**P**oint: State your argumentative claim for this paragraph
**E**vidence: Cite the empirical or scholarly support
**E**xplanation: Explain the logical connection (this is the warrant)
**L**ink: Connect to the overall thesis or transition to the next argument

Ensure every paragraph advances the thesis — it should be impossible to remove a paragraph without weakening your overall argument.

## Common Logical Fallacies in Academic Writing

Identify and avoid these fallacies in your arguments:

### Category 1: Relevance Fallacies

**Ad Hominem** — Attacking the person, not the argument.
> ❌ "Smith's study is unreliable because he works for a pharmaceutical company."
> ✓ "Smith's study may be subject to industry bias; independent replications are needed (Jones, 2022)."

**Appeal to Authority** — Citing authority without evaluating evidence.
> ❌ "Professor X says this treatment works, so it must be true."
> ✓ "Professor X's clinical observations are supported by three RCTs (Smith, 2021; Jones, 2022; Lee, 2023)."

**Appeal to Popularity** — True because widely believed.
> ❌ "Most people believe X, therefore X is true."
> ✓ "Although X is widely assumed, empirical evidence suggests otherwise (Smith, 2023)."

**Straw Man** — Misrepresenting an opponent's argument.
> ❌ "Critics of capitalism want total government control of the economy."
> ✓ "Critics of unregulated capitalism argue that market failures require targeted regulatory interventions (Smith, 2022)."

### Category 2: Inductive Fallacies

**Hasty Generalisation** — Drawing broad conclusions from insufficient evidence.
> ❌ "Three participants reported improved mood; therefore, the intervention is effective."
> ✓ "Preliminary data from three participants suggest possible mood benefits; controlled trials are needed."

**False Cause (Post hoc)** — Correlation assumed to be causation.
> ❌ "Countries with higher chocolate consumption have more Nobel laureates."
> ✓ "While a correlation exists, confounding variables (economic development, education) likely explain the association."

**Slippery Slope** — Assuming extreme consequences without justification.
> ❌ "If we allow assisted dying, soon doctors will euthanise anyone."
> ✓ "Carefully regulated assisted dying frameworks, as in Oregon, have not produced the feared expansions (Battin et al., 2020)."

### Category 3: Structural Fallacies

**False Dichotomy** — Presenting only two options when more exist.
> ❌ "You're either for fossil fuels or you want economic collapse."
> ✓ "The transition to renewable energy can maintain economic stability through phased implementation (IPCC, 2023)."

**Circular Reasoning** — Conclusion restates the premise.
> ❌ "Academic writing is formal because it follows academic conventions, which are formal."
> ✓ "Academic writing employs formal register because it targets expert audiences who expect precision and objectivity (Swales, 1990)."

**Equivocation** — Using a word in two different senses.
> ❌ "Laws of nature are laws; laws require a lawgiver; therefore, nature requires a lawgiver."
> ✓ "Natural laws describe regularities; legal laws prescribe behaviour — the analogy does not hold."

## Counterargument Templates

### Template 1: Acknowledge and Refute
```
While [opponent's position] has merit [in context X], [evidence/reasoning] suggests that [your position] is more compelling in [the broader context/under conditions Y].
```
Example: "While some scholars argue that social media has minimal effect on adolescent wellbeing (Orben & Przybylski, 2019), subsequent analyses accounting for usage patterns and content type reveal more substantial associations (Twenge et al., 2022)."

### Template 2: Concede and Extend
```
[Opponent's position] correctly identifies [partial truth], but fails to account for [overlooked factor], which [changes/nuances/complicates] the conclusion.
```

### Template 3: Limit and Qualify
```
[Opponent's position] may hold under [specific conditions], but does not generalise to [broader context] because [reason].
```

### Template 4: Evidence-Based Rebuttal
```
[Opponent's claim] is contradicted by [specific evidence], which shows [specific finding], with [effect size/statistical measure].
```

## Thesis Statement Construction

A strong academic thesis statement includes:
1. **Topic** — What is being discussed
2. **Claim** — Your position or argument
3. **Reasoning** — Why/how (brief)
4. **Significance** — Why it matters (optional but powerful)

**Weak thesis:** "Social media affects teenagers."
**Strong thesis:** "Sustained social media use exceeding three hours daily is associated with clinically meaningful increases in adolescent depression, particularly among girls, through mechanisms of social comparison and sleep disruption — findings that challenge current self-regulation approaches to digital wellbeing policy."
