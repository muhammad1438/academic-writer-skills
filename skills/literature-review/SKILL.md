# Literature Review

A literature review is not a summary parade — it is a **critical, synthesising argument** that demonstrates your command of the existing discourse and carves out the intellectual space your own research will occupy.

---

## Types of Literature Reviews

| Type | Purpose | Structure |
|---|---|---|
| **Narrative Review** | Broad synthesis and critical discussion of a field | Thematic or chronological; author-led |
| **Systematic Review** | Exhaustive, reproducible survey of all evidence on a question | PRISMA protocol; strict inclusion/exclusion |
| **Scoping Review** | Map the breadth of a topic; identify gaps | PRISMA-ScR; broader scope than systematic |
| **Literature Review Chapter** (Dissertation) | Contextualise your own study; establish the gap | Funnel structure: broad → specific |
| **Integrative Review** | Synthesise diverse methodologies | Theory-building focus |
| **Critical Review** | Evaluate quality and contribution of a body of work | Argumentative; includes critique of methods |

## Narrative Literature Review — Structure

### The Funnel Model (Standard Dissertation Chapter)

```
┌─────────────────────────────────────┐
│  Broad theoretical context          │  (What does the field broadly know?)
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Key themes and debates             │  (What are the major arguments?)
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Specific studies relevant to you   │  (What evidence exists closest to your topic?)
├─────────────────────────────────────┤
│  Research gap                       │  (What is still unknown or contested?)
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
│  Your study's positioning           │  (How does your study address this gap?)
└─────────────────────────────────────┘
```

### Thematic Organisation (Preferred over Chronological)
Organise sources by **theme/concept**, not by author or publication date:
- Group sources that agree with each other
- Group sources that disagree or complicate each other
- Each theme = one section with its own mini-argument

Avoid the "annotated bibliography" mistake: writing a paragraph per source in isolation. Synthesis means showing how sources **talk to each other**.

## Systematic Review — PRISMA Framework

Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) is the international standard for systematic reviews.

### PRISMA Phases

1. **Identification:** Search all relevant databases (PubMed, Web of Science, Scopus, JSTOR, etc.) using pre-defined search strings
2. **Screening (Title & Abstract):** Remove duplicates; screen by title and abstract for relevance
3. **Eligibility (Full Text):** Assess remaining papers against formal inclusion/exclusion criteria
4. **Included:** Final set of papers that meet all criteria

### Documentation Requirements
- **Search strategy:** Must be fully reproducible — list every database, every search term, Boolean operators used, date range
- **PRISMA Flow Diagram:** Visual representation of papers at each stage (required in most journals)
- **Inclusion criteria:** Population, Intervention/exposure, Comparison, Outcome (PICO framework)
- **Exclusion criteria:** State why papers were excluded (wrong population, methodology, date, language)
- **Data extraction table:** Standardised extraction of key variables from each included study

## The Synthesis vs. Summary Distinction

| Summary | Synthesis |
|---|---|
| Reports what one source says | Connects what multiple sources say to each other |
| Source-by-source structure | Theme-by-theme structure |
| "Smith (2019) found that X" | "While Smith (2019) found X, Jones (2021) complicates this by demonstrating Y, a tension also observed by Lee (2022)" |
| Descriptive | Analytical and evaluative |

**The test:** Can you remove any one source from your paragraph without breaking the logic? If yes — you are summarising, not synthesising.

## Identifying and Writing the Research Gap

The research gap is the **most important sentence** in your literature review — it justifies your entire study.

### Types of Research Gaps
- **Empirical gap:** Topic has not been studied in a specific population, context, or time period
- **Methodological gap:** Prior studies use weak or limited methods; a more rigorous approach is needed
- **Theoretical gap:** Existing theories do not explain a particular phenomenon
- **Geographical gap:** Research exists in Western contexts but not in your regional context
- **Temporal gap:** The field has not been updated since significant changes occurred

### Gap-Signalling Phrases
- "To date, there has been little agreement on..."
- "Previous studies have failed to specify..."
- "What is not yet clear is the impact of X on..."
- "No study has examined X in the context of..."
- "While X has been extensively studied, Y remains largely unexplored..."
- "Existing research is limited by its reliance on..."
- "There remains a paucity of longitudinal evidence on..."

## Critical Analysis in the Literature Review

Do NOT simply report findings — evaluate them:
- **Sample limitations:** Small samples, unrepresentative populations, self-selected groups
- **Methodological weaknesses:** Lack of control group, self-report bias, cross-sectional design
- **Theoretical limitations:** Assumes Western frameworks that may not apply universally
- **Temporal issues:** Research is outdated; conditions have changed significantly
- **Conflicting evidence:** Sources contradict each other — note the conflict and analyse why

### Critical Verbs for Evaluation
- challenges, problematises, overlooks, neglects, fails to account for, is limited by, underestimates, overgeneralises, does not distinguish between

## Synthesis Paragraph Structure

**Topic sentence** → State the theme or argument this paragraph addresses

**Evidence 1** → Introduce first source and its finding (paraphrase + cite)

**Evidence 2** → Introduce contrasting or supporting source (paraphrase + cite)

**Analysis** → What does this tension or agreement reveal? Why does it matter?

**Link** → Connect to the broader argument or bridge to the next theme

### Example Synthesis Paragraph
> Organisational commitment has consistently been identified as a key predictor of employee retention (Meyer & Allen, 1991; Mowday et al., 1979). However, more recent empirical work suggests that the relationship is moderated by job autonomy and perceived organisational support (Eisenberger et al., 2020; Rhoades & Eisenberger, 2002). These findings complicate early models, which treated commitment as a relatively stable, individual-level disposition (Allen & Meyer, 1996), by demonstrating that structural and relational workplace factors play a significant role. The implication is that retention strategies focused purely on attitudinal change may be insufficient without corresponding changes to organisational conditions.

## Common Literature Review Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Annotated bibliography structure (one source per paragraph) | Restructure thematically; make sources speak to each other |
| Quoting too heavily instead of synthesising | Paraphrase and attribute; reserve direct quotes for key definitions only |
| Including outdated sources without acknowledging it | Note the date and explain why older foundational work is still relevant |
| No critical evaluation — just description | Add evaluative language; note limitations of each study |
| Missing the research gap | Explicitly state what is unknown; do not leave it implicit |
| No clear organisation (sources listed randomly) | Use headings and thematic structure |
| Including everything you read | Only include sources relevant to your specific research question |

## Review Type Decision Guide

### Choosing Your Review Methodology

```
What is your research question?
  ├─ Broad overview of a field? → Narrative Review
  ├─ Specific, answerable PICO question? → Systematic Review
  ├─ Mapping extent of evidence? → Scoping Review
  ├─ Quantitative synthesis of effect sizes? → Meta-Analysis
  └─ Mixed methods synthesis? → Mixed Methods Review
```

### Narrative Review
**When to use:** Broad topics, introductory sections, theoretical discussions.
**Advantages:** Flexible, covers wide ground, expert interpretation valued.
**Limitations:** Prone to selection bias; less replicable.
**Reporting:** No formal protocol required; cite key sources; acknowledge limitations.

### Systematic Review
**When to use:** Specific clinical or empirical question; evidence synthesis for policy.
**Required elements:**
1. Pre-specified protocol (register on PROSPERO before searching)
2. Comprehensive search strategy (multiple databases, grey literature)
3. Documented inclusion/exclusion criteria
4. Quality assessment of included studies
5. PRISMA reporting (flow diagram + 27-item checklist)

**Search strategy template:**
```
("keyword 1" OR "synonym 1") AND
("keyword 2" OR "synonym 2") AND
("keyword 3" OR "synonym 3")
```
Run in: PubMed, Embase, Cochrane Library, PsycINFO (add field-specific databases).

### Scoping Review
**When to use:** Emerging field; map concepts and evidence types; identify research gaps.
**Different from systematic review:** No quality assessment; broader inclusion.
**Report with:** PRISMA-ScR (Scoping Review extension).

### Meta-Analysis
**When to use:** Sufficient homogeneous studies exist; quantitative synthesis appropriate.
**Required:** Effect sizes and variance measures from each study.
**Report:** Pooled effect size with 95% CI, heterogeneity (I², τ²), funnel plot.

## Synthesis vs. Summary

The central skill in literature reviewing is synthesis — making meaning across sources, not just describing them.

| Summary | Synthesis |
|---------|-----------|
| "Smith (2020) found X" | "Studies consistently show X (Smith, 2020; Jones, 2019)" |
| Each paragraph = one paper | Each paragraph = one theme/finding |
| Papers are described in sequence | Papers compared and contrasted |
| Author's perspective is absent | Your analytical voice is present |
| "Next, Lee (2021) argued..." | "This debate reflects..." |

### Synthesis Sentence Starters
- "Taken together, these studies suggest..."
- "A consistent finding across the literature is..."
- "Contrasting explanations have been offered: [X], while [Y]..."
- "The evidence for X is mixed: [Smith] found Y, while [Jones] found Z..."
- "A gap in the existing research is..."

## Research Gap Identification

After synthesising, explicitly identify gaps:
1. **Population gaps:** Studies focus on Western, WEIRD (Western, Educated, Industrialised, Rich, Democratic) samples
2. **Methodological gaps:** Mostly cross-sectional studies; no longitudinal data
3. **Contextual gaps:** No studies in low-income countries
4. **Theoretical gaps:** No studies testing [specific theory]
5. **Temporal gaps:** No studies since [major event]
6. **Replication gaps:** Key findings not independently replicated
