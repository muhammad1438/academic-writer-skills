# Peer Review Response

The peer review response is one of the most high-stakes documents in academic publishing. A poorly written rebuttal can cause editors to reject a paper that might have been accepted. A masterful response can rescue a paper from rejection.

---

## Understanding the Editor's Decision Letter

### Decision Types and What They Mean

| Decision | What It Means | Typical Revision Scope |
|---|---|---|
| **Accept** | Rare on first submission; paper is accepted as-is | Minor typographical corrections only |
| **Minor Revision** | Accept in principle; small changes needed | 1–2 weeks; targeted fixes |
| **Major Revision** | Accept possible if concerns are adequately addressed | 1–3 months; possibly new analyses |
| **Reject with Invitation to Resubmit** | The paper needs fundamental reworking | Major restructuring; treated as new submission |
| **Reject** | Paper is not suitable for this journal | Consider a different journal |

### Reading the Decision Letter Strategically
1. **Read the editor's letter first, not the reviews** — The editor often summarises the key issues
2. **Identify which concerns the editor highlights** — These carry more weight than reviewer-only concerns
3. **Rank the reviewer comments** — Distinguish mandatory changes from suggestions
4. **Check the deadline** — Most journals give 30–60 days for major revisions; request an extension if needed

## Rebuttal Letter Structure

### Standard Format

**Part 1: Cover Letter to the Editor**

> Dear [Editor Name],
>
> We thank you and the reviewers for the careful and constructive review of our manuscript entitled "[Title]" (Manuscript ID: [ID]). We have carefully considered all comments and have made substantial revisions to the manuscript. We believe the revised manuscript is significantly improved and we hope it is now suitable for publication in [Journal Name].
>
> Below, we provide a detailed point-by-point response to each reviewer's comment. All changes in the revised manuscript are indicated in [red text / tracked changes / a separate document].
>
> We remain available to answer any further questions.
>
> Sincerely,
> [Corresponding Author]

**Part 2: Point-by-Point Responses**

Organise responses under clearly labelled headers:

```
REVIEWER 1

Comment 1:
[Quote the reviewer's comment verbatim]

Response:
[Your response]

Manuscript Change:
[State exactly what was changed and where: "Lines 145–152 in the revised manuscript now read: '...'"]
```

---

## The Point-by-Point Response Template

For every reviewer comment, use this three-part structure:

### Part 1: Acknowledgement
Begin by acknowledging the reviewer's point — even if you disagree with it.
> "We thank the reviewer for this insightful comment."
> "We appreciate the reviewer raising this important issue."
> "We agree with the reviewer that this point required clarification."

### Part 2: Action Taken / Your Response
Explain what you did (or why you respectfully disagree).
> "In response, we have [added a new analysis / revised the Methods section / added Figure X / added a paragraph on page Y]."
> "We respectfully disagree with this interpretation because [reason with evidence]."

### Part 3: Manuscript Change
State precisely where the change appears in the revised manuscript.
> "The revised manuscript now reads (lines 145–152): '[exact new text]'"
> "Table 3 has been added to address this concern, showing [description]."

## The Professional Tone Principle

**Never argue, always acknowledge.** The goal is not to win a debate — it is to address the reviewer's underlying concern, even if you believe they are wrong.

### When the Reviewer is Right
Simply agree, thank them, and describe the change:
> "The reviewer is correct. We have revised the Discussion accordingly (lines 312–318)."

### When the Reviewer is Partially Right
Acknowledge the valid element, address what you disagree with respectfully:
> "We appreciate the reviewer's concern regarding the sample size. We agree that a larger sample would strengthen the study, and we have added this as a limitation (lines 389–392). However, we note that our sample size was calculated a priori using a power analysis targeting 80% power (Methods, lines 89–95), and is consistent with comparable published studies [cite 2–3]."

### When the Reviewer is Wrong
Disagree diplomatically with evidence, never with irritation:
> "We respectfully disagree with the reviewer's characterisation of our analysis as [X]. Our approach follows the procedure described by [Author, Year], which is the standard method for [Y]. We have added a sentence clarifying this rationale (line 201)."

**Avoid:**
- "As we clearly stated in the original manuscript..." (condescending)
- "The reviewer has misunderstood..." (hostile)
- "This comment is outside the scope of our paper..." (dismissive without explanation)

## Handling Specific Challenging Situations

### Contradictory Reviewer Requests
When two reviewers ask for opposite things:
> "Reviewer 1 suggested [X], while Reviewer 2 suggested [Y]. After careful consideration, we have [chosen approach Z / adopted a compromise]. We have [added a paragraph / revised the methods] to address both concerns, as detailed below."

Always explain your reasoning. Do not simply ignore one reviewer.

### Requests for New Experiments You Cannot Perform
Be transparent about constraints:
> "We appreciate the reviewer's suggestion to conduct [X experiment]. This would indeed provide additional validation. However, this experiment falls outside the scope of the current study and our current facilities/timeline/ethics approval. We have added this as a direction for future research (Discussion, lines 398–401) and have acknowledged this as a limitation (lines 419–422)."

### Requests That Would Fundamentally Change the Paper
Acknowledge the value of the suggestion while defending your scope:
> "The reviewer's suggestion to expand the analysis to include [Y population] is scientifically interesting. However, this would substantially alter the scope and focus of the paper. We believe the current focus on [X] is a strength of the paper, as it allows us to... We have clarified the scope and its rationale in the Introduction (lines 34–38)."

## Common Response Phrases

### Acknowledging
- "We thank the reviewer for this valuable comment."
- "We agree with the reviewer that this point needed clarification."
- "The reviewer raises an important concern that we have addressed."
- "This is an insightful observation."

### Agreeing and Changing
- "In response to this comment, we have..."
- "We have revised [section] to address this concern."
- "Following the reviewer's suggestion, we have added..."
- "We have rewritten [paragraph] to clarify this point."

### Disagreeing Diplomatically
- "We respectfully disagree with this interpretation."
- "While we appreciate this suggestion, we believe that..."
- "We note that [counter-evidence]."
- "With respect, we believe this concern is addressed by..."

### Offering Compromise
- "As a compromise, we have..."
- "We have added [X] to partially address this concern while maintaining our original approach because..."

## Tracking Changes Across Revisions

### Best Practices
- **Use tracked changes or red text** in the revised manuscript so reviewers and editors can quickly find changes
- **Number all changes** in your response letter and cross-reference to manuscript line numbers
- **Do not change line numbers** after writing your response — submit the tracked-changes version first to get final line numbers, then update your response letter

### Version Management Checklist
- [ ] Revised manuscript with all changes tracked or highlighted
- [ ] Clean version of revised manuscript (no tracking)
- [ ] Point-by-point response letter
- [ ] Cover letter to editor
- [ ] Any supplementary materials added in revision

## The Appeals Process

### When to Appeal a Rejection
Grounds for appeal:
- **Factual error:** The reviewer made a clear factual mistake that materially affected their recommendation
- **Misunderstanding:** The reviewer fundamentally misunderstood the purpose or methods of the paper
- **Inappropriate reviewer expertise:** The reviewer lacked the necessary expertise to evaluate your work

**Do not appeal because:** you believe the reviewer was too critical, or the standard you think the paper meets is higher than the rejection implies.

### How to Write an Appeal
> "We respectfully appeal the decision to reject manuscript [ID]. We believe the decision was based on a factual misunderstanding in Reviewer 2's assessment. Specifically, Reviewer 2 states [quote], which we respectfully note is incorrect because [specific factual correction with evidence/citation]. We believe that, if this point is reconsidered, the manuscript meets the standards of [Journal]. We would be grateful if the editor would reconsider the manuscript in light of this clarification."

---

## Resubmission Checklist

Before submitting the revised manuscript:
- [ ] Every reviewer comment has been addressed (even if the response is to disagree)
- [ ] All changes are traceable (line numbers, tracked changes, or highlighted text)
- [ ] The response letter is professionally toned throughout
- [ ] The cover letter thanks the editor and summarises the key revisions
- [ ] All new references cited in the response are also added to the reference list
- [ ] No new errors have been introduced by the revisions
- [ ] Word count and format still comply with journal guidelines
- [ ] The response letter is not so long it becomes burdensome (aim for concision)
