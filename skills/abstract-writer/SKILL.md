# Abstract Writer

The abstract is the **vital threshold** to an academic paper — often the sole text read during preliminary literature searches. It functions as a standalone proxy for the entire document. Its structural integrity is paramount.

---

## Two Primary Abstract Types

### 1. Descriptive Abstract
- Functions like a table of contents in paragraph form
- Outlines broad scope and overarching purpose
- **Does NOT include** findings, conclusions, or recommendations
- Length: typically under 100 words
- Used in: humanistic essays, broad theoretical reviews, book proposals
- Purpose: entice the reader to access the full text

### 2. Informational Abstract (Standard for Most Academic Writing)
- Acts as a **comprehensive surrogate** for the full document
- Must stand alone — a reader should understand the study without reading the paper
- Tightly aligned with IMRAD structure
- Length: typically 150–300 words (check journal guidelines)
- Used in: STEM, empirical social sciences, technical reports, journal manuscripts

---

## The Structured Abstract Format

Used by many medical and social science journals (BMJ, Lancet, APA journals):

| Section | Content | ~Word Count |
|---|---|---|
| **Background/Context** | Why does this topic matter? What is the problem? | 30–40 |
| **Objective/Aim** | What did this study set out to do? | 15–25 |
| **Methods** | How was it done? (design, participants, measures, analysis) | 40–60 |
| **Results** | What were the key findings? Include key statistics. | 40–60 |
| **Conclusion** | What do the findings mean? What is the implication? | 20–30 |

---

## The 5-Move Informational Abstract Framework

Even for unstructured abstracts, ensure all 5 rhetorical moves are present:

1. **Establish context / significance** — Why does this matter to the field?
2. **State the problem or gap** — What is unknown or unresolved?
3. **Describe the methodology** — How was the research conducted?
4. **Present the key results** — What was found? (Be specific — include numbers where relevant)
5. **State the implication / conclusion** — What does it mean for the field, practice, or future research?

---

## Step-by-Step Abstract Writing Process

1. **Write the paper first** — The abstract summarises; it cannot be written before the content exists
2. **Identify one sentence for each move** above
3. **Draft without the paper in front of you** — This forces genuine summary
4. **Check:** Does each claim in the abstract appear in the paper? Abstract must not introduce new claims
5. **Remove all citations** — Abstracts should contain no in-text citations (unless journal guidelines require them)
6. **Use past tense** for methods and results ("Data were collected…", "Results showed…")
7. **Use present tense** for established facts and implications ("These findings suggest…")
8. **Avoid first-person** where possible — use "The study," "This paper," "The analysis"
9. **Trim to word limit** — Delete throat-clearing phrases, cut redundancy

---

## Keywords Selection

Keywords enable database indexing and discoverability.

### Rules:
- Typically 4–8 keywords (check journal guidelines)
- Do NOT repeat words already in the title
- Use **controlled vocabulary** where possible:
  - MeSH (Medical Subject Headings) for medicine
  - ERIC thesaurus for education
  - PsycINFO thesaurus for psychology
- Also include relevant free keywords that researchers in the field commonly search
- Move from specific to general (specific concept → broader field)

### Format:
- Listed alphabetically or in order of importance
- Separated by semicolons or commas (per journal guidelines)
- Not capitalised (unless proper nouns)

## Common Abstract Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Too vague — describes the paper without saying what was found | Include specific results; do not just say "results are discussed" |
| Introduces information not in the paper | Remove — the abstract summarises only what exists |
| Contains in-text citations | Remove all citations (unless journal specifically requires them) |
| Uses first-person excessively | Rephrase to "The study," "This paper," "An analysis was conducted" |
| Describes methodology without stating results | Always include key findings |
| Makes claims beyond what the data support | Apply hedging language |
| Exceeds word limit | Cut: remove redundant phrases, combine sentences |
| Written before the paper is finished | Write last — after the full paper is complete |

## Annotated Example — Informational Abstract

> **[Context/Significance]** Burnout among nursing professionals represents a significant threat to both healthcare quality and patient safety, with documented rates increasing sharply since 2019. **[Problem/Gap]** Despite this, few studies have examined the relationship between organisational support structures and burnout rates among nurses in low-resource hospital settings. **[Methods]** This cross-sectional study surveyed 342 registered nurses across six public hospitals in Morocco using the Maslach Burnout Inventory (MBI) and the Survey of Perceived Organisational Support (SPOS). **[Results]** Results indicated that perceived organisational support was significantly and negatively correlated with burnout (r = −0.61, p < 0.001), with nurses in facilities with structured mentorship programmes reporting 38% lower emotional exhaustion scores. **[Conclusion]** These findings suggest that targeted organisational interventions — particularly formal mentorship — may substantially mitigate burnout risk among nursing staff, with implications for hospital management policy across resource-limited health systems.

## Conference Abstract vs. Journal Abstract

| Feature | Conference Abstract | Journal Abstract |
|---|---|---|
| Length | Often 250–300 words (strict limit) | 150–300 words (varies by journal) |
| Results required | Sometimes preliminary results acceptable | Full, definitive results required |
| Structured format | Less common | Often required (especially medical) |
| Purpose | Proposal for presentation | Proxy for published paper |
| Review process | Often double-blind | Part of full-paper peer review |

## Structured Abstract Formats by Discipline

### Clinical Trial Abstract (CONSORT)
Required fields: Background, Objectives, Design, Setting, Participants, Interventions, Main Outcome Measures, Results, Conclusions, Trial Registration.

Example structure:
> **Background:** [Context and rationale]
> **Objectives:** [Primary aim]
> **Design:** [RCT, double-blind, parallel-group]
> **Setting:** [Clinical setting, country]
> **Participants:** [Inclusion/exclusion criteria, n randomised]
> **Interventions:** [Experimental vs. control]
> **Main outcome measures:** [Primary endpoint, timing]
> **Results:** [Primary outcome: RR, 95% CI, p-value]
> **Conclusions:** [Clinical implication]
> **Trial registration:** [ClinicalTrials.gov NCT number]

### Systematic Review Abstract (PRISMA)
Required fields: Background, Objectives, Data Sources, Study Eligibility Criteria, Study Appraisal and Synthesis Methods, Results, Limitations, Conclusions.

### Empirical Psychology Abstract (APA 7th)
Structure: Objective → Participants → Method → Results → Conclusions → Keywords
Word limit: 150–250 words
Keywords: 3–5 terms on line below abstract, not bold

### Humanities Abstract
- No IMRaD structure required
- Describe argument, approach, and contribution
- Mention primary sources or archives used
- State scholarly conversation being entered

### Conference Abstract
- Often shorter (200–300 words)
- Must stand alone (reviewers see only abstract)
- State hypothesis/argument, methods, key findings, significance
- Some conferences require: Introduction, Methods, Results, Discussion (mini-paper)

## Discipline-Specific Vocabulary

### Sciences
- "We investigated...", "We report...", "Results indicate..."
- Report key statistics in abstract if space allows
- State effect sizes and confidence intervals for clinical papers

### Social Sciences
- "This study examines...", "Drawing on [theory]..."
- State theoretical framework
- Describe participant sample briefly

### Humanities
- "This article argues...", "This paper contends...", "I demonstrate..."
- First person often acceptable
- State archival sources or corpus if applicable

## Common Abstract Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---------|-----|
| Too long | Cut background; every sentence must earn its place |
| Missing conclusions | Add: "These findings suggest..." |
| Jargon without definition | Either define or replace with plain term |
| Vague results | Replace "significant results" with actual statistics |
| Passive throughout | Mix active and passive; active for your contributions |
| Missing keywords | Add 3-5 terms below abstract |
