# AI Academic Ethics

The integration of Large Language Models (LLMs) into academic writing has necessitated **immediate and profound policy evolution** across publishers, institutions, and style guide committees. The landscape is evolving rapidly — always verify current policies with your specific institution and target journal.

---

## The Fundamental Rule Across All Publishers

**AI tools cannot be listed as authors or co-authors on any research paper.**

Authorship implies:
- Legal accountability for the work's integrity
- Ethical responsibility for the data and claims
- Ability to assert the integrity of research
- Capacity to hold copyright

AI systems inherently lack all of these. Assigning authorship to an AI is a violation of academic publishing standards regardless of the publisher or discipline.

---

## What AI Can and Cannot Ethically Do

### Generally Acceptable (with disclosure)
- Improving language clarity, grammar, and style
- Translating content from one language to another
- Summarising literature (with human verification of accuracy)
- Generating initial outlines or brainstorming structures
- Checking for logical consistency in arguments
- Formatting references (always verify manually)
- Generating alternative phrasings for a passage you've already written

### Ethically Contested / Requires Explicit Disclosure
- Drafting substantial sections of text (even when heavily revised by the human author)
- Synthesising literature across many sources
- Generating research ideas, hypotheses, or study designs
- Processing or interpreting data

### Generally Not Acceptable
- Having AI write the paper with minimal human intellectual contribution
- Presenting AI-generated arguments as your own original analysis
- Using AI to generate data or fabricate citations (a growing academic fraud risk)
- Submitting AI-generated text without any disclosure

**Key principle:** The human author retains **full, unmitigated accountability** for all AI-generated content included in a submission — for its accuracy, integrity, and originality.

## Mandatory Disclosure Requirements

When AI is used in any meaningful way, disclosure is **absolutely mandatory** across major publishers (Springer Nature / Nature Portfolio, Elsevier, Wiley, Taylor & Francis, IEEE, ACM).

### What Disclosure Must Include
- Name of the AI tool
- Version used
- Extent of application
- Specific tasks performed

### Example Disclosure Statement
> "The authors used ChatGPT 4.0 (OpenAI, 2024) to assist in translating the initial draft from Arabic to English and to improve grammatical clarity in the final manuscript. All scientific content, analysis, and conclusions were generated, verified, and are the sole responsibility of the human authors."

### Where to Place Disclosure
- Methods section (for AI used in data analysis or literature synthesis)
- Acknowledgements section (for AI used in writing assistance or translation)
- Check specific journal guidelines — placement varies

## Citing AI-Generated Content

### APA 7th (Informal / When Output Cannot Be Retrieved)
If an AI conversation cannot be retrieved by readers, treat as personal communication:
> (ChatGPT, personal communication, March 2, 2024)
> Do not include in the reference list.

### When AI Output Is Retrievable (e.g., Shared Link)
> OpenAI. (2024). *ChatGPT* (GPT-4) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com

### Vancouver Adaptation
AI outputs available only to the author (non-retrievable) should be cited as "personal correspondence" and excluded from the formal reference list.

**Note:** Citation conventions for AI are still actively being developed. Always check the most current guidance from your target journal or institution.

## University Policy Landscape

Policy varies significantly by institution and is evolving rapidly. General trends as of 2025–2026:

### Spectrum of Institutional Stances
1. **Full prohibition:** AI use of any kind constitutes academic misconduct (common in examinations and certain assignments)
2. **Permitted with disclosure:** AI may assist but must be disclosed and does not replace the student's own intellectual work
3. **Encouraged with transparency:** AI used as a tool with documented process; the learning outcome must still be demonstrated
4. **Assignment-specific:** Different policies for different tasks within the same course

### Institutions with Published Guidelines (Check for Updates)
- Harvard, MIT, Stanford, Oxford, UCL — all have published AI academic integrity policies
- Most major universities are now updating policies annually
- **Always check your specific institution's current policy — it may have changed**

## AI Detection Tools — What the Research Says

| Tool | Method | Limitations |
|---|---|---|
| **Turnitin AI Detector** | Statistical language pattern analysis | High false positive rates for non-native English writers; does not definitively prove AI use |
| **GPTZero** | Perplexity and burstiness analysis | Unreliable on short texts; false positives common |
| **ZeroGPT** | Pattern detection | Limited accuracy; not peer-reviewed methodology |
| **Copyleaks AI Detector** | Proprietary model | Improving but still not definitive |

### Critical Limitations of AI Detection
- **No tool is definitive.** Detection tools report probabilistic scores, not facts.
- **False positives are documented** — particularly for non-native English speakers, technical writing, and highly structured academic prose
- Detection tools should **not be used as sole evidence** of AI use in academic misconduct proceedings
- AI-generated text that is substantially revised by a human may evade detection entirely
- The field of AI detection is in an arms race with generation capabilities

## Ethical Framework for AI-Assisted Academic Writing

Ask these questions before submitting any AI-assisted work:

1. **Transparency:** Have I disclosed all AI assistance transparently?
2. **Intellectual ownership:** Do the ideas, arguments, and analysis genuinely represent my own thinking?
3. **Accuracy:** Have I verified all facts, citations, and claims generated by the AI? (AI hallucination of sources is a documented, serious risk)
4. **Institutional compliance:** Does my use comply with my institution's current policy?
5. **Publisher compliance:** Does my use comply with the target journal's current policy?
6. **Learning:** Have I actually engaged with the material, or has AI bypassed the learning process?

---

## AI Hallucination — A Critical Academic Risk

AI tools frequently generate **plausible-sounding but entirely fabricated citations**, including:
- Papers that do not exist
- Real authors attributed to papers they did not write
- Incorrect page numbers, volumes, or DOIs
- Misrepresentation of a real paper's actual findings

**Rule:** Never use an AI-generated citation without independently verifying it in a real academic database (Google Scholar, PubMed, Scopus, Web of Science). This is non-negotiable.

---

## Preprint Servers and AI Disclosure

Platforms such as Springer Nature's "In Review" (bioRxiv, medRxiv) require authors to disclose provisional findings clearly, including:
- That the research has not yet undergone formal peer review
- Whether AI tools were used in the research or writing process

Media, policymakers, and the public must understand when research is preliminary and when AI has played a role.

### Disclosure Statement Templates

**For Methods section:**
> "We used [Tool name, version] (Developer, year) to assist with [specific task: initial draft generation / grammar checking / data analysis code / figure creation]. All AI-generated content was reviewed, edited, and verified by the authors. The authors take full responsibility for the integrity of the work."

**For Acknowledgements:**
> "The authors used [Tool] to assist with [task]. The authors reviewed and edited the output and take full responsibility for the content."

**For Cover Letter:**
> "We wish to disclose that AI tools were used during manuscript preparation: [Tool] was used for [purpose]. All content was reviewed and verified by the authorship team."

### What Counts as "Use" Requiring Disclosure

**Must disclose:**
- Generating initial drafts of any section
- Paraphrasing or rewriting substantial passages
- Generating ideas, outlines, or argument structures
- Creating or editing figures (DALL-E, Midjourney)
- Analysing data or generating code used in analysis

**Grey area (disclose to be safe):**
- Grammar and spell checking (most policies: no disclosure needed for basic tools)
- Translation assistance
- Literature search assistance

**Does not require disclosure:**
- Using standard spell-checkers
- Using reference management software (Zotero, Mendeley)
- Using plagiarism detection software

### Citing AI Tools

**APA 7th format:**
> OpenAI. (2025). ChatGPT (Version GPT-4o) [Large language model]. https://chat.openai.com

**MLA 9th format:**
> "Explain the role of epigenetics in aging." ChatGPT, version GPT-4o, OpenAI, 15 Jan. 2025, chat.openai.com.

**IEEE format:**
> OpenAI, "ChatGPT," Large Language Model. [Online]. Available: https://chat.openai.com. Accessed: Jan. 15, 2025.

**Chicago (Notes-Bibliography):**
> OpenAI. 2025. "ChatGPT Response to '[Your Prompt]'." Accessed January 15, 2025. https://chat.openai.com.

### Institutional Policy Lookup

If your institution has its own AI policy, check:
1. Your university's Academic Integrity office website
2. Your department's postgraduate handbook
3. Your supervisor (for thesis work)
4. The specific journal's author guidelines (always the final authority)
