# Academic CV and Job Application Materials

Academic job applications require a distinct set of documents that differ significantly from industry applications. An academic CV can span 5–15 pages, research statements articulate your scholarly trajectory, and teaching philosophies demonstrate pedagogical commitment. This skill covers all components of academic job applications across international contexts.

---

## Academic CV vs. Resume

The academic CV (curriculum vitae) is fundamentally different from an industry resume:

| Feature | Academic CV | Industry Resume |
|---|---|---|
| **Length** | 5–15+ pages (grows throughout career) | 1–2 pages (condensed) |
| **Purpose** | Comprehensive scholarly record | Targeted marketing document |
| **Detail level** | Exhaustive (every publication, talk, service) | Selective (relevant highlights) |
| **Format** | Sections by category; no page limit | Reverse chronological; strict limits |
| **Audience** | Search committees; tenure committees | Hiring managers; recruiters |
| **Scope** | Career-long record | Last 10–15 years |

**When to use each:**
- **Academic positions** (faculty, postdoc, research scientist) → Academic CV
- **Industry R&D** with publications → Modified CV (shorter)
- **Industry/consulting/non-academic** → Resume

## CV Sections

A comprehensive academic CV includes the following sections, ordered by priority for your career stage:

### 1. Header

```
YOUR NAME
Current Title, Institution
Email | Phone | ORCID | Google Scholar | Personal Website
```

**Requirements:**
- Name in 14–16pt bold
- Professional email (institution preferred over personal)
- ORCID iD (essential for publishing)
- Google Scholar profile link (shows citation metrics)
- Personal website or ResearchGate/Academia.edu if active

### 2. Education

List in reverse chronological order:

```
PhD in [Discipline], [Month Year]
[University Name], [City, Country]
Dissertation: "[Title]"
Advisor: [Name], [Institution]
Committee: [Names]
Awards: [Fellowships, honors during degree]
```

**Include:**
- All degrees (Bachelor's onward)
- Dissertation/thesis title (especially for recent PhDs)
- Advisor name (for PhD only; optional after 5+ years)
- Relevant honors, fellowships, awards during degree

### 3. Appointments / Academic Positions

```
Assistant Professor, [Department]
[University], [City, Country]
[Month Year] – Present

Postdoctoral Fellow, [Lab/Department]
[University], [City, Country]
[Month Year] – [Month Year]
Supervisor: [Name]
```

**Format:**
- Reverse chronological order
- Include rank, department, institution, dates
- For postdocs: supervisor name and research focus

### 4. Publications

The most scrutinised section for research positions.

**Formatting by type:**

```
PEER-REVIEWED JOURNAL ARTICLES
[* indicates equal contribution; † indicates corresponding author]

[1] Author A, Author B, Author C†. (Year). Article title.
     Journal Name, Volume(Issue), pages. DOI
     [IF: X.X; Citations: Y]

BOOK CHAPTERS
[2] Author A. (Year). Chapter title. In Editor (Ed.),
    Book Title (pp. XX–XX). Publisher.

PEER-REVIEWED CONFERENCE PROCEEDINGS
[3] Author A, Author B. (Year). Paper title. In
    Conference Name (pp. XX–XX). Publisher.

PREPRINTS
[4] Author A. (Year). Article title. arXiv:XXXX.XXXXX

UNDER REVIEW
[5] Author A. (Year). Article title. Journal Name.
    [Status: Under review / Revise and resubmit]
```

**Organisation strategies:**
- **By type:** Peer-reviewed, book chapters, conference proceedings, preprints
- **By significance:** Highlight high-impact journals with IF or citations
- **Numbered:** Use consistent numbering throughout CV
- **Bold your name** in author list for quick scanning

**Metrics to include (selectively):**
- Impact factor (for high-IF journals)
- Citation count (for highly cited papers)
- Altmetric score (for papers with media attention)

### 5. Grants and Fellowships

```
PRINCIPAL INVESTIGATOR
[Grant Title]
[Funder], [Program]
[Amount], [Dates]
Role: PI (XX% effort)

CO-INVESTIGATOR / COLLABORATOR
[Grant Title]
[Funder], [Amount], [Dates]
Role: Co-I (XX% effort)

FELLOWSHIPS
[Fellowship Name]
[Funder], [Dates]
[Amount and purpose]
```

**Essential elements:**
- Title of grant/fellowship
- Funder (full name, not acronym unless obvious)
- Amount (total award, not just your portion)
- Dates (start and end)
- Your role (PI, Co-I, collaborator, consultant)
- For fellowships: competitiveness (e.g., "awarded to top 5% of applicants")

### 6. Teaching Experience

```
COURSES TAUGHT
[Course Name], [Course Number]
[Institution], [Semester/Year]
Role: Instructor of Record / Teaching Assistant / Guest Lecturer
Enrollment: XX students
Responsibilities: [brief description]

TEACHING DEVELOPMENT
- [Certificate in University Teaching]
- [Teaching workshops completed]
- [Mentoring: undergraduate researchers, graduate students]
```

**For each course, include:**
- Course title and number
- Institution
- Role (Instructor of Record, TA, grader)
- Enrollment size
- Brief description of responsibilities

**For teaching-focused positions:**
- Expand this section significantly
- Include student evaluations (summary statistics)
- List course development and curriculum design
- Highlight innovative pedagogy

### 7. Research Experience

For early-career researchers, this section may precede Appointments:

```
RESEARCH POSITIONS
Research Assistant, [Lab Name]
[Institution], [Dates]
Supervisor: [Name]
Project: [Brief description]

RESEARCH VISITS / COLLABORATIONS
Visiting Researcher, [Institution]
[Dates]
Host: [Name], Purpose: [Brief description]
```

### 8. Service

```
DEPARTMENTAL SERVICE
- [Committee name], [Role], [Dates]
- [Search committee, Task force, etc.]

UNIVERSITY SERVICE
- [Committee or role], [Dates]

PROFESSIONAL SERVICE
- Journal reviewer: [List journals]
- Conference reviewer: [List conferences]
- Editorial board: [Journal, Role, Dates]
- Professional organisation: [Role, Dates]
```

**Why this matters:**
- Demonstrates collegiality and citizenship
- Shows engagement with broader academic community
- Essential for tenure review

### 9. Awards and Honors

```
[Year]  [Award Name], [Awarding Body]
[Year]  [Fellowship/Prize], [Institution/Organization]
[Year]  [Best paper/presentation award], [Conference]
```

**Order:**
- Most prestigious first, or
- Reverse chronological

### 10. Invited Talks and Presentations

```
INVITED TALKS
[Year]  "[Talk Title]." [Department/Seminar Series], [Institution].

CONFERENCE PRESENTATIONS
[Year]  Author A, Author B. "[Title]." [Conference], [City].

POSTER PRESENTATIONS
[Year]  Author A, Author B. "[Title]." [Conference], [City].
```

**Distinguish:**
- Invited talks (prestigious; you were asked)
- Contributed talks (you submitted abstract)
- Poster presentations

### 11. Professional Memberships

```
- [Professional Organization], Member, [Year]–Present
- [Specialized society], [Role if any], [Year]–Present
```

### 12. References

```
REFERENCES

[Name], [Title]
[Department]
[Institution]
[Email] | [Phone]
Relationship: [Dissertation chair / Postdoc supervisor / Collaborator]

[Repeat for 3 references]
```

**Reference selection:**
- 3 references standard (some positions request 4–5)
- Include at least one from each significant position
- Ask permission before listing
- Provide updated CV and job description to referees

## Research Statement

A research statement articulates your scholarly identity: what you've done, what you're doing, and what you will do. It demonstrates to search committees that you have a coherent, fundable research program.

### Purpose and Audience

**Who reads it:**
- Faculty search committees
- Postdoc supervisors
- Funding agencies (adapted format)

**What they look for:**
- Clear intellectual agenda
- Independence from advisor (for junior candidates)
- Feasibility and funding potential
- Fit with department priorities
- Trajectory toward significant contributions

### Structure: Past → Present → Future

**Typical length:** 2–4 pages (check position requirements)

**Paragraph-level structure:**

1. **Opening paragraph (Hook)**
   - Your core research question or problem
   - Why it matters (significance)
   - Your unique angle or approach

2. **Past research (1–2 paragraphs)**
   - What you've accomplished
   - Key findings and their impact
   - Skills and methods you've developed
   - Connect to your overall research agenda

3. **Current research (1 paragraph)**
   - What you're working on now
   - Preliminary results or progress
   - How it extends your past work

4. **Future research (2–3 paragraphs)**
   - What you will do in the next 3–5 years
   - Specific aims or projects
   - Methodology overview
   - Expected contributions and impact
   - Funding potential (mention specific funders)

5. **Closing paragraph (Fit)**
   - Why this institution/department
   - Potential collaborators
   - Resources that would support your work

### What Search Committees Look For

| Criterion | How to Demonstrate |
|---|---|
| **Originality** | Novel questions, innovative methods, fresh perspectives |
| **Significance** | Addressing important problems; potential for impact |
| **Feasibility** | Realistic scope; preliminary data; appropriate methods |
| **Independence** | Distinct from advisor; own intellectual identity |
| **Fundability** | Clear fit with funder priorities; realistic budget |
| **Fit** | Complements existing department strengths; fills gaps |

### Common Mistakes

| Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|
| Too broad / unfocused | Narrow to a coherent program; connect projects thematically |
| Too narrow / one-project | Show trajectory beyond dissertation; multiple project lines |
| Too technical for broad audience | Write for intelligent non-specialists; define jargon |
| No future vision | 40–50% should be forward-looking |
| Ignoring fit | Research the department; mention specific colleagues, resources |
| Rehashing dissertation | Brief summary of past; focus on direction and growth |
| Missing funding potential | Mention specific funders and grant mechanisms |

## Teaching Philosophy

A teaching philosophy (or teaching statement) articulates your approach to teaching and learning. It demonstrates pedagogical awareness and commitment to student development.

### Purpose and Audience

**Who reads it:**
- Search committees (especially teaching-focused positions)
- Teaching award committees
- Promotion and tenure committees

**What they look for:**
- Reflective practice (not just what you do, but why)
- Evidence of effectiveness
- Commitment to inclusive pedagogy
- Specific strategies and examples
- Alignment with institutional mission

### Structure Options

**Option 1: Thematic**
- Opening: Your core teaching values
- Theme 1: How you teach (methods)
- Theme 2: How students learn (learning theory)
- Theme 3: How you assess (evaluation)
- Closing: Teaching goals and development

**Option 2: Narrative**
- Opening: A teaching moment that shaped your philosophy
- Your evolution as a teacher
- Core principles with examples
- Assessment and improvement approach
- Closing: Future teaching goals

**Option 3: Question-based**
- What do I believe about how students learn?
- How do I create an inclusive learning environment?
- What teaching methods do I use and why?
- How do I assess student learning?
- How do I continue developing as a teacher?

### Evidence-Based Approach

Avoid vague statements; ground claims in evidence:

| Vague | Evidence-Based |
|---|---|
| "I am passionate about teaching." | "I developed a new lab curriculum that increased student engagement scores by 25%." |
| "I use active learning." | "I use think-pair-share and peer instruction in every lecture, which has improved exam performance by 15% on conceptual questions." |
| "I value diversity." | "I incorporate primary literature from researchers of diverse backgrounds and use case studies relevant to underrepresented student populations." |
| "I seek student feedback." | "I administer mid-semester evaluations and adjust my teaching in response; in my most recent course, I added review sessions based on student requests." |

### Inclusive Pedagogy

Most institutions now expect evidence of inclusive teaching:

- **Course content:** Diverse voices in readings; global perspectives
- **Teaching methods:** Universal Design for Learning; multiple ways to participate
- **Assessment:** Varied assessment types; transparent criteria
- **Classroom climate:** Ground rules for discussion; addressing microaggressions
- **Mentoring:** Supporting students from underrepresented backgrounds

### Length

1–2 pages standard. Teaching-focused positions may allow 3 pages.

## Diversity / Inclusion Statement

Increasingly required, especially at US institutions. This statement demonstrates your commitment to equity and your understanding of systemic barriers in academia.

### Structure and Content

**1. Understanding of diversity issues (1 paragraph)**
- What diversity means in your field
- Specific barriers or inequities you've observed or studied
- Personal connection (optional; don't force it)

**2. Your contributions and actions (2–3 paragraphs)**
- Teaching: inclusive pedagogy, diverse content, mentoring
- Research: community-engaged research, diverse participant recruitment
- Service: outreach, committee work, advocacy
- Personal: experiences with diverse communities

**3. Future plans (1 paragraph)**
- Specific actions you will take
- How you'll continue learning and developing

### Authentic Approach

**Do:**
- Be specific about actions you've taken
- Show ongoing learning and reflection
- Connect to your field and research
- Acknowledge complexity and systemic issues

**Don't:**
- Claim to "not see color" or other forms of colorblindness
- Tokenize students from underrepresented groups
- Claim to have solved all bias issues
- Use generic statements that could apply to anyone

## Cover Letters for Academic Jobs

Academic cover letters differ from industry cover letters in length, detail, and purpose.

### Structure

**Length:** 1.5–2 pages (some institutions allow 3)

**Paragraph structure:**

1. **Opening (1 paragraph)**
   - Position you're applying for
   - Where you saw it advertised
   - Your current position and status

2. **Research paragraph(s) (2–3 paragraphs)**
   - Your research program (condensed from research statement)
   - Key accomplishments and impact
   - Future directions and funding potential
   - Fit with department strengths

3. **Teaching paragraph (1 paragraph)**
   - Courses you can teach
   - Teaching experience and effectiveness
   - New courses you could develop
   - Fit with department's teaching needs

4. **Fit paragraph (1 paragraph)**
   - Why this institution specifically
   - Potential collaborators (name them)
   - Resources that support your work
   - Contribution to department priorities

5. **Closing (brief)**
   - Reference to enclosed materials
   - Availability for interviews
   - Contact information

### Customisation Per Institution

Each cover letter must be tailored:

| Institution Type | Emphasis |
|---|---|
| **Research university (R1)** | Research 60–70%; fit with research priorities; external funding |
| **Teaching-focused (liberal arts)** | Teaching 50–60%; small class pedagogy; mentorship |
| **Comprehensive university** | Balance teaching and research; service to institution |
| **Community college** | Teaching 70%+; diverse student populations; practical applications |

### Difference from Industry Cover Letters

| Feature | Academic | Industry |
|---|---|---|
| **Length** | 1.5–2 pages | 1 page max |
| **Detail** | Specific research accomplishments | Quantified business impact |
| **Tone** | Scholarly but accessible | Professional and concise |
| **Customisation** | Essential; mention specific faculty | Company-level; not individuals |
| **Attachments** | CV, research statement, teaching statement, diversity statement, references | Resume only unless requested |

## Tenure Dossier

A tenure dossier (or promotion file) compiles evidence of your contributions across research, teaching, and service. Requirements vary by institution, but core components include:

### What It Includes

1. **External Review Letters**
   - 4–8 letters from scholars at peer institutions
   - You typically nominate reviewers; administration may add others
   - Letters assess significance and impact of your work

2. **Candidate's Personal Statement**
   - Narrative of your contributions
   - Research trajectory and impact
   - Teaching philosophy and evidence
   - Service contributions

3. **Research Documentation**
   - Complete publication list with citation counts
   - Grants received and submitted
   - Graduate students advised and placements
   - Invited talks and recognition

4. **Teaching Documentation**
   - Courses taught with enrollments
   - Student evaluation summaries (quantitative and qualitative)
   - Teaching awards
   - Curriculum development
   - Student outcomes (theses completed, placements)

5. **Service Documentation**
   - Departmental, university, and professional service
   - Leadership roles
   - Outcomes of service activities

6. **Supporting Materials**
   - Selected publications (2–5 key works)
   - Sample syllabi
   - Letters from students, collaborators

## Country-Specific Requirements

### United States

**Standard application package:**
- CV (comprehensive)
- Cover letter (1.5–2 pages)
- Research statement (2–4 pages)
- Teaching statement (1–2 pages)
- Diversity statement (increasingly required)
- 3–5 letters of recommendation
- Writing sample / selected publications

**Timeline:**
- Faculty positions: Apply August–November for positions starting following August
- Postdocs: Year-round; most advertised 6–12 months before start

### United Kingdom

**Standard application package:**
- CV (more concise than US; 4–6 pages)
- Cover letter (1–2 pages)
- Research plan (often 1–2 pages, focused on future work)
- Teaching statement (if teaching involved)
- References (2–3; often contacted before interview)

**Key differences:**
- More emphasis on impact and public engagement
- Research Excellence Framework (REF) considerations
- Teaching Fellow vs. Lecturer distinction

### European Union

**Standard application package:**
- CV (Europass format sometimes required, but tailored academic CV often preferred)
- Cover letter (1 page)
- Research statement (varies by country/position)
- Teaching statement (if applicable)
- References (2–3)

**Country variations:**
- **Germany:** Habilitation or equivalent required for full professor; Juniorprofessor positions for early-career
- **France:** Competitive selection process (concours) for many positions
- **Netherlands:** Hierarchy from Assistant Professor to Full Professor; tenure track increasingly common

### Australia

**Standard application package:**
- CV
- Cover letter addressing selection criteria
- Separate statement addressing each selection criterion (explicit requirement)
- Research statement
- Teaching statement
- References (3)

**Key differences:**
- Selection criteria must be addressed explicitly (often 1 page per criterion)
- ARC (Australian Research Council) funding record important
- Strong emphasis on impact and engagement

## Common Mistakes

| Document | Mistake | Fix |
|---|---|---|
| **CV** | Too long for career stage | New PhD: 3–5 pages; Assistant Prof: 5–10; Associate: 10–15 |
| **CV** | Missing metrics | Include citation counts, impact factors, grant amounts |
| **CV** | Inconsistent formatting | Use a template; maintain consistent style throughout |
| **Research Statement** | Too focused on past | 40–50% should be future plans |
| **Research Statement** | No fit with institution | Research the department; mention specific faculty |
| **Research Statement** | Generic; could be for any job | Tailor each statement to the specific position |
| **Teaching Philosophy** | No evidence | Include specific examples and outcomes |
| **Teaching Philosophy** | Too abstract | Ground in actual courses and student interactions |
| **Cover Letter** | Generic salutation | Address search committee by name if possible |
| **Cover Letter** | Repeating CV | Tell a story; highlight; don't list |
| **Diversity Statement** | Generic; could apply to anyone | Specific actions you've taken; ongoing learning |

### Research Statement Template (3 pages)

**Page 1: Introduction and Past Research**

Paragraph 1 (Opening): State your core research question or problem. Explain why it matters—significance to your field and to broader society. Present your unique angle or approach.

Paragraph 2 (Past Research Overview): Summarize your most significant past research contributions. Focus on impact, not just activities. What did you discover? What methods did you develop? How has your work influenced the field?

Paragraph 3 (Past Research Detail): Dive deeper into 1–2 key projects. Present specific findings. Connect to your overall research agenda. Demonstrate methodological expertise.

**Page 2: Current and Future Research**

Paragraph 4 (Current Research): What are you working on now? What questions are you addressing? Share preliminary results or progress. Explain how this extends your past work.

Paragraph 5 (Future Research Aims): Lay out your research agenda for the next 3–5 years. Present 2–3 specific aims or projects. Explain methodology briefly. State expected contributions.

Paragraph 6 (Future Research Impact): Address the significance of your planned research. Who will benefit? What fields will it influence? Connect to broader societal impact.

**Page 3: Feasibility, Funding, and Fit**

Paragraph 7 (Feasibility and Resources): Demonstrate that your plans are achievable. Mention preliminary data, collaborators, required resources, timeline.

Paragraph 8 (Funding Potential): Identify specific funders and grant mechanisms relevant to your research. Mention past success with similar funders if applicable.

Paragraph 9 (Fit with Institution): Why this department? Name specific colleagues whose work complements yours. Mention relevant centers, institutes, or facilities. Connect to institutional priorities.

### Teaching Philosophy Template (2 pages)

Paragraph 1 (Core Values): Open with your fundamental beliefs about teaching and learning. What do you want students to achieve? How do you believe learning happens?

Paragraph 2 (Teaching Methods): Describe your primary teaching methods and why you use them. Connect to learning theory or pedagogical research. Give a specific example.

Paragraph 3 (Creating Inclusive Environments): Explain how you create an inclusive learning environment. Discuss diverse perspectives in course content, varied assessment methods, and support for underrepresented students.

Paragraph 4 (Assessment): How do you assess student learning? Why these methods? How do you use assessment to improve your teaching?

Paragraph 5 (Evidence of Effectiveness): Share evidence that your teaching is effective—student outcomes, evaluation scores, student comments, specific examples of student success.

Paragraph 6 (Development and Goals): How do you continue developing as a teacher? What are your future teaching goals? What new methods or courses do you want to develop?
