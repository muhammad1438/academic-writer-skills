# Grant Proposal

A grant proposal is simultaneously a scientific document, a persuasive argument, and a strategic communication with a specific review panel. Understanding the funding body's values and review criteria is as important as the science itself.

---

## Grant Types Comparison

| Funder | Key Grant Types | Review Criteria | Word/Page Limits |
|---|---|---|---|
| **NIH (US)** | R01, R21, R03, K awards, F awards | Significance, Investigators, Innovation, Approach, Environment (scored 1–9) | 12 pages (R01 Research Strategy) |
| **NSF (US)** | Standard, CAREER, collaborative | Intellectual Merit + Broader Impacts (equal weight) | 15 pages (Project Description) |
| **EU Horizon Europe** | ERC Starting/Consolidator/Advanced, MSCA, collaborative | Excellence, Impact, Implementation | Varies by scheme (15–50 pages) |
| **Wellcome Trust (UK)** | Discovery Research, Investigator Awards | Scientific quality, career stage, team, environment | Varies by scheme |
| **ERC** | StG, CoG, AdG, PoC, Synergy | Scientific excellence only (no socioeconomic impact required) | 15 pages (B2 section) |
| **UKRI / MRC / BBSRC** | Standard research grants, fellowships | Timeliness, quality, team, value for money | Typically 6–8 pages |

## Anatomy of a Grant Proposal

### Universal Sections (Most Funders)

| Section | Purpose | Key Principle |
|---|---|---|
| **Abstract / Project Summary** | 1-page standalone overview | Write last; must work independently |
| **Specific Aims / Objectives** | What you will do and why | The make-or-break page (NIH) |
| **Background / Significance** | Why this problem matters | Gap → impact → your solution |
| **Innovation** | What is genuinely new | Must be scientifically novel, not incremental |
| **Approach / Methods** | How you will do it | Feasibility + rigor + contingency plans |
| **Budget Justification** | Why each cost is necessary | Every line must be justified |
| **Biosketches / CVs** | Team credentials | Highlight relevant expertise and track record |
| **Facilities & Resources** | What environment you have | Demonstrates feasibility |
| **References** | Literature cited | Keep focused; reviewers notice padding |

## NIH-Specific: The Specific Aims Page

The Specific Aims page is **the most critical page in an NIH application** — it is the only section every reviewer reads before scoring. A compelling Specific Aims page can carry a mediocre application; a weak one can sink an outstanding one.

### Structure of a Strong Specific Aims Page (1 page exactly)

**Paragraph 1 — The Hook (2–3 sentences)**
Open with the significance: the disease burden, the scientific problem, the unmet need. Make the stakes clear immediately.

**Paragraph 2 — The Gap (2–3 sentences)**
Describe the specific gap in knowledge that your project addresses. Be precise: "While X has been established, the mechanism by which Y leads to Z remains unknown."

**Paragraph 3 — Your Solution (2–3 sentences)**
State your central hypothesis and the overall objective. Introduce your preliminary data briefly to establish feasibility: "Our preliminary data demonstrate that..."

**The Aims (2–3 bullet aims)**
Each aim should:
- Be achievable in the project period
- Be partially independent (failure of one should not collapse the others)
- Map clearly to the hypothesis
- Have a specific, measurable outcome

**Final Paragraph — Impact (2–3 sentences)**
State what successful completion will contribute. Connect to longer-term goals. "The proposed research is significant because it will establish the first mechanistic understanding of X, with direct implications for Y."

### NIH Scoring Criteria (1–9 scale, lower = better)
- **Significance:** Does this address an important problem? Will it advance the field?
- **Investigators:** Are the PI and team appropriately trained and positioned?
- **Innovation:** Does the project employ novel concepts, approaches, or methods?
- **Approach:** Is the strategy well-reasoned? Are limitations acknowledged? Are contingency plans provided?
- **Environment:** Does the setting contribute to the project's success?

## NSF-Specific: The Two Mandatory Review Criteria

NSF gives **equal weight** to both criteria. Many applicants neglect Broader Impacts, which is a fatal mistake.

### Intellectual Merit
- How important is the proposed activity to advancing knowledge?
- Is the plan well-conceived and well-organised?
- Is the applicant qualified?
- Are the resources adequate?

### Broader Impacts
NSF requires you to address how the project will benefit society beyond scientific discovery. Reviewers look for **specific, credible plans**, not vague statements.

Strong Broader Impacts examples:
- Training underrepresented groups in STEM
- Developing open-source tools or datasets for the community
- K-12 outreach programmes with named partners
- Policy translation plans with named stakeholder organisations
- International collaboration that builds capacity in developing countries

**Avoid:** "Results will be published in journals and presented at conferences." This is expected of all research and is NOT a Broader Impact.

## Writing the Significance Section

The Significance section answers: **Why does this matter?**

### The Gap → Impact → Solution Framework

1. **Establish the problem:** What is the disease burden, social problem, or scientific gap?
   > "Alzheimer's disease affects 50 million people worldwide, and no disease-modifying treatment currently exists."

2. **Identify the specific gap:** What specific knowledge is missing that, if addressed, would move the field forward?
   > "The precise role of tau propagation in early-stage cognitive decline remains poorly understood, limiting the development of targeted therapeutics."

3. **Introduce your approach:** Why is your approach well-positioned to address this gap?
   > "The proposed study leverages a novel mouse model that recapitulates early-stage tau pathology with unprecedented fidelity..."

## Common Reviewer Criticisms — and How to Preempt Them

| Criticism | Prevention Strategy |
|---|---|
| "The hypothesis is not clearly stated" | State the central hypothesis explicitly in one sentence in the Aims |
| "The approach is too ambitious for the timeline" | Show phased aims; include realistic milestones; cite team track record |
| "Lack of preliminary data" | Dedicate 1–2 pages to preliminary data; include figures |
| "The significance is overstated" | Hedge appropriately; do not claim more than the data support |
| "Alternative approaches are not discussed" | Include "Potential Problems and Alternative Approaches" subsection |
| "The team lacks expertise in X" | Add collaborator with that expertise; address directly |
| "Budget is not well justified" | Every line item must link to a specific aim or activity |
| "The innovation is incremental" | Clearly state what has not been done before and why your approach is different |

## Budget Narrative Templates

### Personnel
> "Dr [Name], Principal Investigator (20% effort, $X/year): Dr [Name] will provide overall scientific direction, supervise personnel, and lead data analysis. This effort level is required to maintain the scientific momentum of the project."

### Supplies
> "Laboratory supplies ($X/year): Consumables required for [specific assays], including [list]. This estimate is based on our preliminary data indicating [X units] per experiment at [$Y] per unit."

### Travel
> "Domestic travel ($X/year): Two domestic conference presentations per year to disseminate findings (registration $X, airfare $X, hotel $X × 4 nights)."

## Logic Model Framework (Foundations and EU Grants)

Many foundation and EU Horizon grants require a Logic Model showing the causal chain from inputs to outcomes.

| Component | Definition | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Inputs** | Resources you bring | Funding, team expertise, existing data, institutional support |
| **Activities** | What you will do | Data collection, analysis, workshops, publications |
| **Outputs** | Direct products | Papers, datasets, trained researchers, policy briefs |
| **Short-term Outcomes** | Changes within 1–2 years | New knowledge, increased capacity, awareness |
| **Long-term Outcomes** | Changes within 3–5 years | Policy change, clinical translation, field transformation |
| **Impact** | Broader societal change | Improved health outcomes, reduced inequality |

## 8 Fatal Flaws in Grant Proposals

| Flaw | Description |
|---|---|
| **1. Unclear hypothesis** | Aims are activities, not testable claims |
| **2. No preliminary data** | Reviewers cannot assess feasibility |
| **3. Over-ambitious scope** | Too much work for the budget and timeframe |
| **4. Neglected Broader Impacts (NSF)** | Treating it as an afterthought |
| **5. Poor budget justification** | Line items without rationale |
| **6. Ignoring review criteria** | Not structuring the application around the scoring rubric |
| **7. Weak team** | PI lacks relevant experience; no collaborators named for gaps |
| **8. No contingency plans** | Assumes everything will work; does not address risk |
