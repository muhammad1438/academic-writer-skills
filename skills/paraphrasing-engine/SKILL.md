# Paraphrasing Engine

Academic integrity is the bedrock of scholarly communication. The ability to paraphrase, summarise, and quote correctly is one of the most important skills in all academic writing — and one of the most commonly done wrong.

---

## The Four Types of Plagiarism

| Type | Description | Example |
|---|---|---|
| **Direct / Verbatim** | Copying exact words without quotation marks and citation | Pasting a sentence from a source unchanged |
| **Mosaic / Patchwriting** | Mixing original words with synonyms without changing structure; keeping sentence architecture while swapping some words | Changing 3–4 words in an original sentence |
| **Paraphrase Plagiarism** | Restructuring a sentence but staying too close to the source's logic and phrasing | Changing passive to active but keeping all the same ideas in the same order |
| **Self-Plagiarism** | Reusing substantial portions of one's own previously submitted or published work without disclosure | Copying paragraphs from your previous essay into a new paper |

**Common knowledge** does not require citation: well-established facts known by any educated person in the field (e.g., "The Second World War ended in 1945"). When in doubt, cite.

---

## The 6-Step Paraphrasing Process

1. **Read** the original passage carefully until you fully understand it
2. **Close** the source — do not look at it while writing
3. **Write** the idea entirely in your own words from memory
4. **Compare** your version to the original — check that you have changed both the wording AND the sentence structure
5. **Add a citation** — paraphrasing still requires attribution
6. **Verify** — your version should not share more than 2–3 consecutive words with the original

## Paraphrasing Techniques

### Technique 1: Change Sentence Structure
- Original: "The researchers found that students who slept fewer than six hours performed significantly worse on memory tasks."
- Paraphrase: "Memory performance was significantly impaired among students who reported sleeping less than six hours, according to the researchers."

### Technique 2: Change Word Class (Nominalise or De-nominalise)
- Original: "The team *investigated* the long-term *effects* of pollution."
- Paraphrase: "A long-term *investigation* into the *effect* of pollution was carried out by the research team."

### Technique 3: Change Active ↔ Passive
- Active: "Smith (2020) argues that economic growth causes inequality."
- Passive: "It has been argued that inequality is a consequence of economic growth (Smith, 2020)."

### Technique 4: Chunk and Reorder
Break a multi-sentence passage into its core ideas, then recombine them in a different logical order.

### Technique 5: Change Perspective / Syntactic Focus
Shift what is in the subject position: Move the phenomenon, result, or concept into focus, rather than the researchers.

## What Is NOT Acceptable Paraphrasing

**Patchwriting** — the most common academic integrity violation:
> Original: "Climate change poses an existential threat to coastal ecosystems and the communities that depend on them."
> Patchwrite (WRONG): "Climate change presents an existential danger to coastal ecosystems and the people who rely on them." ✗

This is NOT a paraphrase — it merely replaces synonyms while keeping the identical structure and logic. It constitutes plagiarism even with a citation.

> Correct paraphrase: "Coastal communities and the marine ecosystems upon which they depend face severe long-term risks from climate change." ✓

## Summary vs. Paraphrase

| | Paraphrase | Summary |
|---|---|---|
| Length | Similar length to original | Much shorter than original |
| Detail | Preserves most details | Retains only key ideas |
| Scope | Covers one passage or argument | Covers an entire section, article, or chapter |
| Citation | Required | Required |
| Use case | When specific ideas matter | When overall argument matters |

## Quoting Correctly

### When to Quote (Rather Than Paraphrase)
- The author's exact phrasing is important (a definition, a key term, a famous formulation)
- The precise wording is itself the object of analysis (literary/humanities papers)
- Changing the words would lose critical nuance
- You are providing evidence of exactly what was said (legal, historical)

### When to Paraphrase Instead
- The idea matters more than the specific words
- You are synthesising multiple sources
- The original is overly technical or verbose
- In STEM writing — paraphrase is generally preferred over quotation

### Integrating Short Quotes
Quotes must be **grammatically integrated** into your sentence:
- ✓ Smith (2020) argues that economic inequality represents "one of the defining challenges of our era" (p. 14).
- ✗ Economic inequality is a major issue. "One of the defining challenges of our era." (Smith, 2020, p. 14)

### Block Quotes
Used when the quotation is **40+ words (APA)** or **4+ lines of prose (MLA)**:
- Indent the entire block (typically 0.5 inch / 1.27 cm)
- No quotation marks around the block
- Citation appears after the final punctuation
- Use sparingly — over-use of block quotes suggests inability to synthesise

### Modifying Quotes
- **Ellipsis [...]** — Use to omit irrelevant middle content (never omit to distort meaning)
- **Square brackets [ ]** — Use to clarify a pronoun, change a verb tense for grammatical fit, or add a word for clarity
- Example: "She [the lead researcher] concluded that the effect was significant."

### Citing Secondary Sources
Use only when the original source is inaccessible:
- APA: (Original Author, Year, as cited in Secondary Author, Year)
- MLA: (qtd. in Secondary Author page)
- Chicago: Note: "Original Author, Title, quoted in Secondary Author, Title, page."

## Similarity Score Guidance (Turnitin, iThenticate)

- There is **no universal "safe" percentage** — context matters entirely
- A 30% similarity score from properly cited block quotes may be acceptable
- A 5% similarity score from uncited matched passages may be plagiarism
- **Read the full report**, not just the score — check what the matches are and where they appear
- Common legitimate matches: reference list, institution name, methodology boilerplate (acceptable)
- Common problem matches: body paragraphs, argument sentences (investigate carefully)

### Detection Tools
- **Turnitin:** Most widely used; checks against student paper database, internet, published works
- **iThenticate:** Used for journal manuscripts and dissertations
- **Grammarly Plagiarism:** Checks against web content
- **Unicheck, Copyleaks, PlagScan:** Alternative platforms

All tools flag textual similarity — **they do not determine plagiarism**. Human judgment is still required.

## Source Integration Ladder

Moving from minimal to deep integration with source material:

### Level 1: Direct Quotation (Minimal integration)
Use when: Exact wording is legally/terminologically significant, or particularly memorable.
> According to Smith (2023), "artificial intelligence represents the most significant technological shift since the industrial revolution" (p. 45).

When NOT to quote: Statistics, common knowledge, information available in many sources.

### Level 2: Close Paraphrase (Surface restructuring)
Warning: Often leads to patchwriting. Avoid.
Original: "The results show a significant increase in depression among adolescents who use social media more than three hours daily."
Close paraphrase (BAD): "The findings demonstrate a notable rise in depression in teenagers who spend over three hours on social media per day."
Why bad: Same structure, synonyms substituted.

### Level 3: True Paraphrase (Full restructuring)
True paraphrase (GOOD): "Adolescent depression correlates with extended social media use, with meaningful effects appearing at three or more hours of daily engagement (Smith, 2023)."
Why good: Different sentence structure, different entry point, same meaning, cited.

### Level 4: Summary (Condensed meaning)
Use when: You need to convey the gist of a longer passage or entire paper.
"Smith (2023) found that prolonged social media use — defined as three or more hours daily — significantly increases depression risk in adolescents, with effects persisting after controlling for baseline mood."

### Level 5: Synthesis (Cross-source integration)
Use when: Multiple sources address the same issue.
"Multiple studies have linked heavy social media use to adolescent depression (Smith, 2023; Jones & Lee, 2022; Patel, 2021), though the threshold for harm varies across studies."

## Quote vs. Paraphrase Decision Tree

```
Is the exact wording legally or terminologically essential?
  YES → Quote (with page number)
  NO ↓

Is this a foundational definition in the field?
  YES → Quote or close paraphrase with acknowledgement
  NO ↓

Are you reporting statistics?
  YES → Paraphrase (statistics are not copyrightable)
  NO ↓

Is the passage more than 50 words?
  YES → Summarise
  NO ↓

→ True paraphrase (restructure, cite, move on)
```

## Patchwriting Detection

Patchwriting signs (avoid these patterns):
1. **Synonym substitution only:** same structure, different words
2. **Moving clauses:** same ideas, reordered
3. **String of paraphrases:** each sentence tracks one source sentence
4. **Chunk lifting:** 5+ words identical to original

Test yourself: Cover the source. Can you write the paraphrase from memory? If yes, it's genuine integration.

## Plagiarism vs. Paraphrase vs. Common Knowledge

| Content Type | Citation needed? |
|-------------|-----------------|
| Direct quotation | Yes (author, year, page) |
| Close paraphrase | Yes (author, year) |
| True paraphrase of specific claim | Yes (author, year) |
| Summary of a specific paper | Yes (author, year) |
| Common knowledge (easily verifiable fact) | No |
| Your own original analysis | No |
| Information available in 5+ sources | Usually no |
